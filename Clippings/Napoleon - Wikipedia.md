**Napoleon Bonaparte** [^1] (born **Napoleone di Buonaparte**;[^7] [^2] 15 August 1769 – 5 May 1821), later known by his [regnal name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regnal_name "Regnal name") **Napoleon I**, was [Emperor of the French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emperor_of_the_French "Emperor of the French") from 18 May 1804 until his [first abdication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon%27s_first_abdication "Napoleon's first abdication") in 1814, with a brief restoration during the [Hundred Days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hundred_Days "Hundred Days") in 1815. He rose to prominence as a [general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_of_division "General of division") during the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") and led [a series of military campaigns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_career_of_Napoleon "Military career of Napoleon") across Europe and North Africa during the [French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolutionary_and_Napoleonic_Wars "French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars"). As a statesman, he implemented numerous legal and administrative reforms in France and Europe.

Born on the island of [Corsica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsica "Corsica") to a family of Italian origin, Napoleon moved to mainland France in 1779 and was commissioned as an officer in the [French Royal Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Royal_Army "French Royal Army") in 1785. He supported the French Revolution in 1789 and promoted its cause in Corsica. He rose rapidly through the ranks after winning the [siege of Toulon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Toulon_\(1793\) "Siege of Toulon (1793)") in 1793 and defeating royalist insurgents in Paris on [13 Vendémiaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/13_Vend%C3%A9miaire "13 Vendémiaire") in 1795. In 1796, he commanded [a military campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_campaign_of_1796%E2%80%931797 "Italian campaign of 1796–1797") against the [Austrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_monarchy "Habsburg monarchy") and their Italian allies in the [War of the First Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition "War of the First Coalition"), scoring decisive victories and becoming a national hero. He led an [invasion of Egypt and Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Egypt_and_Syria "French invasion of Egypt and Syria") in 1798, which served as a springboard to political power. In November 1799, Napoleon engineered the [Coup of 18 Brumaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Brumaire "Coup of 18 Brumaire") against the [French Directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Directory "French Directory") and became First Consul of the Republic. He won the [Battle of Marengo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Marengo "Battle of Marengo") in 1800, which secured France's victory in the [War of the Second Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Second_Coalition "War of the Second Coalition"), and in 1803, he [sold the territory of Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase "Louisiana Purchase") to the United States. In December 1804, Napoleon [crowned himself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation_of_Napoleon "Coronation of Napoleon") Emperor of the French, further expanding his power.

The breakdown of the [Treaty of Amiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Amiens "Treaty of Amiens") led to the [War of the Third Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Third_Coalition "War of the Third Coalition") by 1805. Napoleon shattered the coalition with a decisive victory at the [Battle of Austerlitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Austerlitz "Battle of Austerlitz"), which led to the [dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire "Dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire"). In the [War of the Fourth Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Fourth_Coalition "War of the Fourth Coalition"), Napoleon defeated [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Kingdom of Prussia") at the [Battle of Jena–Auerstedt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jena%E2%80%93Auerstedt "Battle of Jena–Auerstedt") in 1806, marched his *[Grande Armée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e "Grande Armée")* into Eastern Europe, and defeated the [Russians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") in 1807 at the [Battle of Friedland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Friedland "Battle of Friedland"). Seeking to extend his [trade embargo against Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_System "Continental System"), Napoleon invaded the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") and installed his brother [Joseph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte "Joseph Bonaparte") as [King of Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Spain "King of Spain") in 1808, provoking the [Peninsular War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsular_War "Peninsular War"). In 1809, the Austrians again challenged France in the [War of the Fifth Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Fifth_Coalition "War of the Fifth Coalition"), in which Napoleon solidified his grip over Europe after winning the [Battle of Wagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram "Battle of Wagram"). In the summer of 1812, he launched [an invasion of Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia "French invasion of Russia"). After victory at the [Battle of Borodino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino "Battle of Borodino"), he briefly [occupied Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_occupation_of_Moscow "French occupation of Moscow") before conducting a catastrophic retreat of his army that winter. In 1813, [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussia "Prussia") and [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_Empire "Austrian Empire") joined Russia in the [War of the Sixth Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Sixth_Coalition "War of the Sixth Coalition"), in which Napoleon was decisively defeated at the [Battle of Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Leipzig "Battle of Leipzig"). The coalition [invaded France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_in_north-east_France_\(1814\) "Campaign in north-east France (1814)") and captured Paris, [forcing Napoleon to abdicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Fontainebleau_\(1814\) "Treaty of Fontainebleau (1814)") in April 1814. They exiled him to the Mediterranean island of [Elba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elba "Elba") and restored the [Bourbons to power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration_in_France "Bourbon Restoration in France"). Ten months later, Napoleon escaped from Elba with a thousand men and marched on Paris, again taking control of the country. His opponents responded by forming a [Seventh Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seventh_Coalition "Seventh Coalition"), which defeated him at the [Battle of Waterloo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo "Battle of Waterloo") in June 1815. Napoleon was exiled to the remote island of [Saint Helena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Helena "Saint Helena") in the South Atlantic, where he died of stomach cancer in 1821, aged 51.

Napoleon is considered one of the great military commanders in history, and [Napoleonic tactics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_tactics "Napoleonic tactics") are still studied at military schools worldwide. [His legacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legacy_of_Napoleon "Legacy of Napoleon") endures through the modernizing legal and administrative reforms he enacted in France and Western Europe, embodied in the [Napoleonic Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Code "Napoleonic Code"). He established a system of public education,[^8] abolished the vestiges of [feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism"),[^9] [emancipated Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_and_the_Jews "Napoleon and the Jews") and other religious minorities,[^10] abolished the [Spanish Inquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition "Spanish Inquisition"),[^11] enacted the principle of [equality before the law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_before_the_law "Equality before the law") for an emerging middle class,[^12] and centralized state power at the expense of religious authorities.[^13] His conquests acted as a catalyst for political change and the development of [nation states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state"). However, he is controversial because of his role in wars which devastated Europe, his [looting of conquered territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_looting_of_art "Napoleonic looting of art"), and his mixed record on civil rights. He abolished the free press, ended directly elected representative government, exiled and jailed critics of his regime, [reinstated slavery in French colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinstatement_of_slavery_by_Napoleon "Reinstatement of slavery by Napoleon"), banned the entry of [black people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_people "Black people") and [mulattos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mulatto "Mulatto") into France, reduced the civil rights of women and children, reintroduced a hereditary monarchy and nobility,[^14] [^15] [^16] and violently repressed popular uprisings against his rule.[^17]

## Early life

Napoleon's family was of Italian origin. His paternal ancestors, the Buonapartes, descended from a minor [Tuscan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuscany "Tuscany") noble family who emigrated to [Corsica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsica "Corsica") in the 16th century. His maternal ancestors, the Ramolinos, descended from a noble family from [Lombardy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lombardy_\(historical_region\) "Lombardy (historical region)").[^18] Napoleon's parents, [Carlo Maria Buonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlo_Buonaparte "Carlo Buonaparte") and [Maria Letizia Ramolino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letizia_Bonaparte "Letizia Bonaparte"), lived in the [Maison Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maison_Bonaparte "Maison Bonaparte") in [Ajaccio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajaccio "Ajaccio"), where Napoleon was born on 15 August 1769. He had an elder brother, [Joseph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte "Joseph Bonaparte"), and six younger siblings: [Lucien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucien_Bonaparte "Lucien Bonaparte"), [Elisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elisa_Bonaparte "Elisa Bonaparte"), [Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Bonaparte "Louis Bonaparte"), [Pauline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauline_Bonaparte "Pauline Bonaparte"), [Caroline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caroline_Bonaparte "Caroline Bonaparte"), and [Jérôme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte "Jérôme Bonaparte").[^19] Five more siblings were stillborn or did not survive infancy.[^20] Napoleon was baptized as a [Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") under the name *Napoleone di Buonaparte*. In his youth, his name was also spelled as *Nabulione*, *Nabulio*, *Napolionne*, and *Napulione*.[^21]

Napoleon was born one year after the [Republic of Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa") ceded Corsica to France through the [Treaty of Versailles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles_\(1768\) "Treaty of Versailles (1768)").[^22] [^3] His father supported [Pasquale Paoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasquale_Paoli "Pasquale Paoli") during the [Corsican war of independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conquest_of_Corsica "French conquest of Corsica") against France. After the Corsican defeat at the [Battle of Ponte Novu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Ponte_Novu "Battle of Ponte Novu") in 1769 and Paoli's exile to Britain, Carlo became friends with the French governor [Charles Louis de Marbeuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Louis_de_Marbeuf "Charles Louis de Marbeuf"), who became his patron and a godfather to Napoleon.[^26] [^27] With Marbeuf's support, Carlo was named Corsican representative to the court of [Louis XVI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XVI "Louis XVI"), and Napoleon obtained a royal bursary to a military academy in mainland France.[^28] [^29]

![Half-length portrait of a wigged middle-aged man with a well-to-do jacket. His left hand is tucked inside his waistcoat.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Carlo_Buonaparte.jpg/250px-Carlo_Buonaparte.jpg)

Napoleon's father, Carlo Buonaparte, fought for Corsican independence under Pasquale Paoli. After their defeat, he eventually became the island's representative to Louis XVI 's court.

The dominant influence of Napoleon's childhood was his mother, whose firm discipline restrained a rambunctious child.[^28] Later in life, Napoleon said, "The future destiny of the child is always the work of the mother." [^30] Napoleon's noble, moderately affluent background afforded him greater opportunities to study than were available to a typical Corsican of the time.[^31]

In January 1779, aged 9, Napoleon moved to the French mainland and enrolled at a religious school in [Autun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autun "Autun") to improve his French,[^32] his mother tongues being [Corsican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsican_language "Corsican language") and Italian.[^33] [^34] Although he eventually became fluent in French, he spoke it with a Corsican accent, and his French spelling was poor.[^35] In May, he transferred to the military academy at [Brienne-le-Château](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brienne-le-Ch%C3%A2teau "Brienne-le-Château") where he was routinely bullied by his peers for his accent, birthplace, short stature, mannerisms, and poor French.[^33] He became reserved and melancholic, applying himself to reading. An examiner observed that Napoleon "has always been distinguished for his application in mathematics. He is fairly well acquainted with history and geography... This boy would make an excellent sailor".[^4] [^37]

One story of Napoleon at the school is that he led junior students to victory against senior students in a snowball fight, which allegedly showed his leadership abilities.[^38] But the story was only told after Napoleon had become famous.[^39] In his later years at Brienne, Napoleon became an outspoken [Corsican nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsican_nationalism "Corsican nationalism") and admirer of Paoli.[^40]

In September 1784 Napoleon was admitted to the *[École militaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_militaire "École militaire")* in Paris where he trained to become an artillery officer. He excelled at mathematics and read widely in geography, history and literature. However, he was poor at French and German.[^41] His father's death in February 1785 cut the family income and forced him to complete the two-year course in one year. In September he was examined by the famed scientist [Pierre-Simon Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace") and became the first Corsican to graduate from the *École militaire*.[^42] [^43]

## Early career

### Return to Corsica

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Henri_F%C3%A9lix_Emmanuel_Philippoteaux_-_Portrait_of_Napoleon_Bonaparte.jpg/250px-Henri_F%C3%A9lix_Emmanuel_Philippoteaux_-_Portrait_of_Napoleon_Bonaparte.jpg)

Bonaparte, aged 23, as lieutenant-colonel of a battalion of Corsican Republican volunteers. Portrait made in 1835 by Henri Félix Emmanuel Philippoteaux.

Upon graduating in September 1785, Bonaparte was commissioned a [second lieutenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_lieutenant "Second lieutenant") in [*La Fère* artillery regiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1st_Artillery_Regiment_\(France\) "1st Artillery Regiment (France)").[^44] He served in [Valence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence,_Dr%C3%B4me "Valence, Drôme") and [Auxonne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auxonne "Auxonne") until after the outbreak of the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") in 1789 but spent long periods of leave in Corsica, which fed his Corsican nationalism.[^45] [^46] In September 1789 he returned to Corsica and promoted the French revolutionary cause. Paoli returned to the island in July 1790, but he had no sympathy for Bonaparte, as he deemed his father a traitor for having deserted the cause of Corsican independence.[^47] [^48]

Bonaparte plunged into a complex three-way struggle among royalists, revolutionaries, and Corsican nationalists. He became a supporter of the [Jacobins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobins "Jacobins") and joined the pro-French Corsican Republicans who opposed Paoli's policy and his aspirations to secede.[^49] He was given command over a battalion of Corsican volunteers and promoted to captain in the regular army in 1792, despite exceeding his leave of absence and a dispute between his volunteers and the French garrison in [Ajaccio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajaccio "Ajaccio").[^50] [^51]

In February 1793, Bonaparte took part in the failed [French expedition to Sardinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_expedition_to_Sardinia "French expedition to Sardinia"). Following allegations that Paoli had sabotaged the expedition and that his regime was corrupt and incompetent, the French [National Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Convention "National Convention") outlawed him. In early June, Bonaparte and 400 French troops failed to capture Ajaccio from Corsican volunteers, and the island came under the control of Paoli's supporters. When Bonaparte learned that the Corsican assembly had condemned him and his family, the Buonapartes fled to [Toulon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toulon "Toulon") on the French mainland.[^52] [^53]

### Siege of Toulon

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Napoleon_%C3%A0_Toulon_par_Edouard_Detaille.jpg/250px-Napoleon_%C3%A0_Toulon_par_Edouard_Detaille.jpg)

Bonaparte at the Siege of Toulon, 1793, by Édouard Detaille

Bonaparte returned to his regiment in [Nice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nice "Nice") and was made captain of a coastal [battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artillery_battery "Artillery battery").[^54] In July 1793 he published a pamphlet, *[Le souper de Beaucaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_souper_de_Beaucaire "Le souper de Beaucaire")* (Supper at [Beaucaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaucaire,_Gard "Beaucaire, Gard")), demonstrating his support for the National Convention which was heavily influenced by the Jacobins.[^55] [^56]

In September, with the help of his fellow Corsican [Antoine Christophe Saliceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Christophe_Saliceti "Antoine Christophe Saliceti"), Bonaparte was appointed artillery commander of the republican forces sent to recapture the port of Toulon, which was occupied by allied forces.[^57] He quickly increased the available artillery and proposed a plan to capture a hill fort where republican guns could dominate the city's harbour and force the allies to evacuate. The successful assault on the position on 16–17 December led to the capture of the city.[^58]

Toulon brought Bonaparte to the attention of powerful men including [Augustin Robespierre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin_Robespierre "Augustin Robespierre"), the younger brother of [Maximilien Robespierre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre "Maximilien Robespierre"), a leading Jacobin. He was promoted to brigadier general and put in charge of defences on the Mediterranean coast. In February 1794, he was made artillery commander of the [Army of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_Italy_\(France\) "Army of Italy (France)") and devised plans to attack the [Kingdom of Sardinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia "Kingdom of Sardinia").[^59] [^60]

The French army carried out Bonaparte's plan in the [Second Battle of Saorgio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Saorgio "Second Battle of Saorgio") in April 1794 and then advanced to seize [Ormea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ormea "Ormea") in the mountains. From Ormea, it headed west to outflank the Austro-Sardinian positions around [Saorge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saorge "Saorge"). After this campaign, Augustin Robespierre sent Bonaparte on a mission to the [Republic of Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa") to determine the country's intentions towards France.[^61] [^62]

### 13 Vendémiaire

![Etching of a street, there are many pockets of smoke due to a group of republican artillery firing on royalists across the street at the entrance to a building](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Charles_Monnet_-_The_Journ%C3%A9e_of_13_Vend%C3%A9miaire.jpg/250px-Charles_Monnet_-_The_Journ%C3%A9e_of_13_Vend%C3%A9miaire.jpg)

Journée du 13 Vendémiaire, artillery fire in front of the Church of Saint-Roch, Paris, Rue Saint-Honoré

After the [Fall of Maximilien Robespierre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Maximilien_Robespierre "Fall of Maximilien Robespierre") in July 1794, Bonaparte's association with leading Jacobins made him politically suspect to the new regime. He was arrested on 9 August but released two weeks later.[^63] [^64] [^65] He was asked to draw up plans to attack Italian positions as part of France's war with Austria, and in March 1795 he took part in an expedition to take back Corsica from the British, but the French were repulsed by the [Royal Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy "Royal Navy").[^66]

From 1794, Bonaparte was in a romantic relationship with [Désirée Clary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A9sir%C3%A9e_Clary "Désirée Clary"), whose sister [Julie Clary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_Clary "Julie Clary") had married Bonaparte's elder brother Joseph.[^67] [^68] In April 1795 Bonaparte was assigned to the [Army of the West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_the_West_\(France\) "Army of the West (France)"), which was engaged in the [War in the Vendée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_the_Vend%C3%A9e "War in the Vendée") —a civil war and royalist counter-revolution in the [Vendée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vend%C3%A9e "Vendée") region. As an infantry command, it was a demotion from artillery general, and he pleaded poor health to avoid the posting.[^69] During this period, he wrote the romantic novella *[Clisson et Eugénie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clisson_et_Eug%C3%A9nie "Clisson et Eugénie")*, about a soldier and his lover, in a clear parallel to Bonaparte's own relationship with Clary.[^70]

In August, he obtained a position with the Bureau of Topography, where he worked on military planning.[^70] On 15 September, he was removed from the list of generals in regular service for refusing to serve in the Vendée campaign.[^71] He sought a transfer to [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") to offer his services to Sultan [Selim III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selim_III "Selim III"). The request was eventually granted, but he never took up the post.[^72] [^73]

On 3 October royalists in Paris declared a rebellion against the National Convention.[^74] [Paul Barras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Barras "Paul Barras"), a leader of the [Thermidorian Reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidorian_Reaction "Thermidorian Reaction"), knew of Bonaparte's military exploits at Toulon and made him second in command of the forces defending the convention in the [Tuileries Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuileries_Palace "Tuileries Palace"). Bonaparte had seen the massacre of the king's [Swiss Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Guards "Swiss Guards") during the [Insurrection of 10 August 1792](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurrection_of_10_August_1792 "Insurrection of 10 August 1792") there three years earlier and realized that artillery would be the key to its defence. He ordered a young cavalry officer, [Joachim Murat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joachim_Murat "Joachim Murat"), to seize cannons, and Bonaparte deployed them in key positions. On 5 October 1795— *13 Vendémiaire An IV* in the [French Republican calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_calendar "French Republican calendar") —he fired on the rebels with canister rounds (described by [Thomas Carlyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle "Thomas Carlyle") as "the whiff of [grapeshot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapeshot "Grapeshot") ") [^75]. About 300 to 1,400 rebels died in the uprising.[^74] [^76] [^77] Bonaparte's role in defeating the rebellion earned him and his family the patronage of the new government, the [French Directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Directory "French Directory").[^78] On 26 October, he was promoted to commander of the [Army of the Interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_the_Interior "Army of the Interior"), and in January 1796, he was appointed head of the Army of Italy.[^79]

Within weeks of the *Vendémiaire* uprising*,* Bonaparte was romantically involved with [Joséphine de Beauharnais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9phine_de_Beauharnais "Joséphine de Beauharnais"), the former mistress of Barras. Josephine had been born in the French colonies in the [Lesser Antilles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesser_Antilles "Lesser Antilles"), and her family owned slaves on [sugar plantations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean "Sugar plantations in the Caribbean").[^80] The couple married on 9 March 1796 in a civil ceremony.[^81] Bonaparte began to habitually style himself "Napoleon Bonaparte" rather than using the Italian form "Napoleone di Buonaparte." [^82] [^7] [^83]

### First Italian campaign

![A three-quarter-length depiction of Bonaparte, with black tunic and leather gloves, holding a standard and sword, turning backwards to look at his troops](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg/250px-1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg)

Bonaparte at the Pont d'Arcole, by Baron Antoine-Jean Gros, ( c. 1801 ), Musée du Louvre, Paris

Two days after the marriage, Bonaparte left Paris to take command of the Army of Italy. He went on the offensive, hoping to defeat the Kingdom of Sardinia in [Piedmont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piedmont "Piedmont") before their Austrian allies could intervene. In a series of victories during the [Montenotte campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montenotte_campaign "Montenotte campaign"), he knocked the Piedmontese out of the war in two weeks.[^84] The French then focused on the Austrians, [laying siege to Mantua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Mantua_\(1796%E2%80%931797\) "Siege of Mantua (1796–1797)"). The Austrians launched offensives against the French to break the siege, but Bonaparte defeated every relief effort, winning the [Battle of Castiglione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Castiglione "Battle of Castiglione"), the [Battle of Bassano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Bassano "Battle of Bassano"), the [Battle of Arcole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Arcole "Battle of Arcole"), and the [Battle of Rivoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Rivoli "Battle of Rivoli"). The French triumph at Rivoli in January 1797 led to the collapse of the Austrian position in Italy. At Rivoli, Austria lost 43% of its soldiers dead, wounded or taken prisoner.[^85] [^86]

The French then invaded the heartlands of the [House of Habsburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Habsburg "House of Habsburg"). French forces in southern Germany had been defeated by [Archduke Charles, Duke of Teschen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archduke_Charles,_Duke_of_Teschen "Archduke Charles, Duke of Teschen") in 1796, but Charles withdrew his forces to protect [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna "Vienna") after learning of Bonaparte's assault. In their first encounter, Bonaparte pushed Charles back and advanced deep into Austrian territory after winning the [Battle of Tarvis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Tarvis_\(1797\) "Battle of Tarvis (1797)") in March 1797. Alarmed by the French thrust that reached [Leoben](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leoben "Leoben"), about 100 km from Vienna, the Austrians sued for peace.[^87] [^88] The preliminary [peace of Leoben](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Leoben "Peace of Leoben"), signed on 18 April, gave France control of most of northern Italy and the [Low Countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Countries "Low Countries") and promised to partition the [Republic of Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice") with Austria.[^89] Bonaparte marched on Venice and [forced its surrender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Republic_of_Venice#12_May_1797:_the_Fall_of_the_Venetian_Republic "Fall of the Republic of Venice"), ending 1,100 years of Venetian independence. He authorized the French to loot treasures such as the [Horses of Saint Mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horses_of_Saint_Mark "Horses of Saint Mark").[^90] [^91]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Napoleon_at_the_Battle_of_Rivoli.jpg/250px-Napoleon_at_the_Battle_of_Rivoli.jpg)

Napoleon at the Battle of Rivoli by Henri Félix Emmanuel Philippoteaux, 1844

In this Italian campaign, Bonaparte's army captured 150,000 prisoners, 540 cannons, and 170 [standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag "Flag"). The French army fought 67 actions and won 18 [pitched battles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitched_battle "Pitched battle") through superior artillery technology and Bonaparte's tactics.[^92] Bonaparte extracted an estimated 45 million French pounds from Italy during the campaign, another 12 million pounds in precious metals and jewels, and more than 300 paintings and sculptures.[^93]

During the campaign, Bonaparte became increasingly influential in French politics. He founded two newspapers: one for the troops in his army and one for circulation in France.[^94] The royalists attacked him for looting Italy and warned that he might become a dictator.[^95] Bonaparte sent General [Charles-Pierre Augereau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Pierre_Augereau "Charles-Pierre Augereau") to Paris to support a *coup d'état* that purged royalists from the legislative councils on 4 September—the [Coup of 18 Fructidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Fructidor "Coup of 18 Fructidor"). This left Barras and his republican allies in control again, but more dependent upon Bonaparte, who finalized peace terms with Austria by the [Treaty of Campo Formio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Campo_Formio "Treaty of Campo Formio").[^96] Bonaparte returned to Paris on 5 December 1797 as a hero.[^97] He met [Charles Maurice de Talleyrand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand "Charles Maurice de Talleyrand"), France's foreign minister, and took command of the [Army of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_England "Army of England") for the planned invasion of Britain.[^98]

### Egyptian expedition

![Person on a horse looks towards a giant statue of a head in the desert, with a blue sky](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Bonaparte_ante_la_Esfinge%2C_por_Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me.jpg/250px-Bonaparte_ante_la_Esfinge%2C_por_Jean-L%C3%A9on_G%C3%A9r%C3%B4me.jpg)

Bonaparte Before the Sphinx ( c. 1886 ) by Jean-Léon Gérôme, Hearst Castle

After two months of planning, Bonaparte decided that France's naval strength was not yet sufficient to confront the [Royal Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Navy "Royal Navy"). He decided on a military expedition to seize [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Egypt "Ottoman Egypt") and thereby undermine [Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain "Kingdom of Great Britain") 's access to its [trade interests in India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Company_rule_in_India#Trade "Company rule in India").[^44] Bonaparte wished to establish a French presence in the Middle East and join forces with [Tipu Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipu_Sultan "Tipu Sultan"), the [Sultan of Mysore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mysore "Kingdom of Mysore"), an enemy of the British.[^99] Bonaparte assured the Directory that "as soon as he had conquered Egypt, he will establish relations with the Indian princes and, together with them, attack the English in their possessions".[^100] The Directory agreed in order to secure a trade route to the [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent").[^101]

In May 1798, Bonaparte was elected a member of the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences"). His Egyptian expedition included a group of 167 scientists, with mathematicians, naturalists, chemists, and [geodesists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy") among them. Their discoveries included the [Rosetta Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta_Stone "Rosetta Stone"), and their work was published in the *[Description de l'Égypte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Description_de_l%27%C3%89gypte "Description de l'Égypte")* in 1809.[^102] En route to Egypt, Bonaparte reached [Hospitaller Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospitaller_Malta "Hospitaller Malta") on 9 June 1798, then controlled by the [Knights Hospitaller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller "Knights Hospitaller"). Grand Master [Ferdinand von Hompesch zu Bolheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Hompesch_zu_Bolheim "Ferdinand von Hompesch zu Bolheim") surrendered after token resistance, and Bonaparte captured an important naval base with the loss of only three men.[^103]

![Cavalry battlescene with pyramids in background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Louis-Fran%C3%A7ois_Baron_Lejeune_001.jpg/330px-Louis-Fran%C3%A7ois_Baron_Lejeune_001.jpg)

Battle of the Pyramids on 21 July 1798 by Louis-François, Baron Lejeune, 1808

Bonaparte and his expedition eluded pursuit by the Royal Navy and landed at [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria") on 1 July.[^44] He fought the [Battle of Shubra Khit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Shubra_Khit "Battle of Shubra Khit") against the [Mamluks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamluk "Mamluk"), Egypt's ruling military caste. This helped the French practise their defensive tactic for the [Battle of the Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Pyramids "Battle of the Pyramids") on 21 July, about 24 km (15 mi) from the [pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids "Egyptian pyramids"). Bonaparte's forces of 25,000 roughly equalled those of the Mamluks' Egyptian cavalry. Twenty-nine French [^104] and approximately 2,000 Egyptians were killed. The victory boosted the French army's morale.[^105]

On 1 August 1798 the British fleet under [Sir Horatio Nelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson "Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson") captured or destroyed all but two vessels of the French fleet in the [Battle of the Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Nile "Battle of the Nile"), preventing Bonaparte from strengthening the French position in the Mediterranean.[^106] His army had succeeded in a temporary increase of French power in Egypt, though it faced repeated uprisings.[^107] In early 1799 he moved an army into the [Ottoman province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilayah "Wilayah") of Damascus (Syria and [Galilee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilee "Galilee")). Bonaparte led these 13,000 French soldiers in the conquest of the coastal towns of [Arish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arish "Arish"), [Gaza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_City "Gaza City"), [Jaffa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaffa "Jaffa"), and [Haifa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haifa "Haifa").[^108] The [attack on Jaffa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jaffa "Siege of Jaffa") was particularly brutal. Bonaparte discovered that many of the defenders were former prisoners of war, ostensibly on parole, so he ordered the garrison and some 1,500–5,000 prisoners to be executed by bayonet or drowning.[^109] [^110] [^111] Men, women, and children were robbed and murdered for three days.[^112]

Bonaparte began with an army of 13,000 men. 1,500 were reported missing, 1,200 died in combat, and thousands perished from disease—mostly [bubonic plague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubonic_plague "Bubonic plague"). He failed to [reduce the fortress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Acre_\(1799\) "Siege of Acre (1799)") of [Acre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre,_Israel "Acre, Israel"), so he marched his army back to Egypt in May. Bonaparte was alleged to have ordered plague-stricken men to be poisoned with opium to speed the retreat.[^113] Back in Egypt on 25 July, Bonaparte defeated an [Ottoman amphibious invasion at Abukir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Abukir_\(1799\) "Battle of Abukir (1799)").[^114]

Bonaparte stayed informed of European affairs. He learned that France had suffered a [series of defeats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaigns_of_1799_in_the_French_Revolutionary_Wars "Campaigns of 1799 in the French Revolutionary Wars") in the [War of the Second Coalition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_Second_Coalition "War of the Second Coalition").[^115] On 24 August 1799, fearing that the Republic's future was in doubt, he took advantage of the temporary departure of British ships from French coastal ports and set sail for France, despite the fact that he had received no explicit orders from Paris.[^116] The army was left in the charge of [Jean-Baptiste Kléber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Kl%C3%A9ber "Jean-Baptiste Kléber").[^117]

## Ruler of France

![Bonaparte in a simple general uniform in the middle of a scrum of red-robbed members of the Council of Five Hundred](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Bouchot_-_Le_general_Bonaparte_au_Conseil_des_Cinq-Cents.jpg/250px-Bouchot_-_Le_general_Bonaparte_au_Conseil_des_Cinq-Cents.jpg)

General Bonaparte surrounded by members of the Council of Five Hundred during the Coup of 18 Brumaire, by François Bouchot

### 18 Brumaire

Unknown to Bonaparte, the Directory had sent him orders to return from Egypt with his army to ward off a possible invasion of France, but these messages never arrived.[^115] By the time he reached Paris in October, France's situation had been improved by a series of victories. The republic, however, was bankrupt, and the ineffective Directory was unpopular.[^118] Despite the failures in Egypt, Bonaparte returned to a hero's welcome. The Directory discussed Bonaparte's desertion but was too weak to punish him.[^115]

Bonaparte formed an alliance with Talleyrand and leading members of the [Council of Five Hundred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Five_Hundred "Council of Five Hundred") and Directory—Lucien Bonaparte, [Emmanuel Joseph Sieyès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Joseph_Siey%C3%A8s "Emmanuel Joseph Sieyès"), [Roger Ducos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Ducos "Roger Ducos") and [Joseph Fouché](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fouch%C3%A9 "Joseph Fouché") —to overthrow the government. On 9 November 1799 (*18 Brumaire* according to the revolutionary calendar), the conspirators launched a coup and the following day, backed by grenadiers with fixed bayonets, forced the Council of Five Hundred to dissolve the Directory and appoint Bonaparte, Sieyès and Ducos provisional consuls.[^119] [^120]

### French Consulate

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_Portrait_de_Napol%C3%A9on_Bonaparte_en_premier_consul.jpg/250px-Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres%2C_Portrait_de_Napol%C3%A9on_Bonaparte_en_premier_consul.jpg)

Bonaparte, First Consul, by Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres. Posing the hand inside the waistcoat was often used in portraits of rulers to indicate calm and stable leadership.

On 13 December, Bonaparte introduced the [Constitution of the Year VIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Year_VIII "Constitution of the Year VIII"), under which three consuls were appointed for 10 years.[^121] Real power lay with Bonaparte as first consul, and his preferred candidates [Cambacérès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques-R%C3%A9gis_de_Cambac%C3%A9r%C3%A8s "Jean-Jacques-Régis de Cambacérès") and [Charles-François Lebrun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Fran%C3%A7ois_Lebrun "Charles-François Lebrun") were appointed as second and third consuls who only had an advisory role. The constitution also established a [Legislative Body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corps_l%C3%A9gislatif "Corps législatif") and [Tribunate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribunat "Tribunat") which were selected from indirectly elected candidates, and a [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A9nat_conservateur "Sénat conservateur") and [Council of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conseil_d%27%C3%89tat "Conseil d'État") which were effectively nominated by the executive.[^122] The constitution was approved by plebiscite on 7 February 1800. The official count was over three million in favour and 1,562 against. Lucien, however, had doubled the count of the "yes" vote to give the false impression that a majority of those eligible to vote had approved the constitution.[^123] [^124]

Historians have variously described Bonaparte's regime as "dictatorship by plebiscite",[^124] "absolutist rule decked out in the spirit of the age",[^125] and "soft despotism".[^126] Local and regional administration was reformed to concentrate power in the central government,[^127] censorship was introduced, and most opposition newspapers were closed down to stifle dissent.[^128] Royalist and regional revolts were dealt with by a combination of amnesties for those who lay down their arms and brutal repression of those who continued to resist.[^129] [^130] [^131] Bonaparte also improved state finances by securing loans under a promise to defend private property, raising taxes on tobacco, alcohol and salt, and extracting levies from France's satellite republics.[^132]

Bonaparte believed that the best way to secure his regime was by a victorious peace.[^133] In May 1800, he led an army across the Swiss Alps into Italy, aiming to surprise the Austrian armies that had reoccupied the peninsula when Bonaparte was still in Egypt. After a difficult crossing over the Alps,[^5] the French captured Milan on 2 June.[^135] [^136] The French confronted an Austrian army under [Michael von Melas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_von_Melas "Michael von Melas") at the [battle of Marengo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Marengo "Battle of Marengo") on 14 June.[^135] [^136] The Austrians fielded about 30,000 soldiers while Bonaparte commanded 24,000 troops.[^137] The Austrians' initial attack surprised the French who were gradually driven back.[^138] Late in the afternoon, however, a full division under French General [Louis Desaix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Desaix "Louis Desaix") arrived on the field and reversed the tide of the battle. The Austrian army fled leaving behind 14,000 casualties.[^139] The following day, the Austrians signed an armistice and agreed to abandon northern Italy.[^139]

When peace negotiations with Austria stalled, Bonaparte reopened hostilities in November. A French army under General [Jean Victor Marie Moreau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Victor_Marie_Moreau "Jean Victor Marie Moreau") swept through Bavaria and scored an overwhelming victory over the Austrians at the [battle of Hohenlinden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Hohenlinden "Battle of Hohenlinden") in December. The Austrians capitulated and signed the [Treaty of Lunéville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Lun%C3%A9ville "Treaty of Lunéville") in February 1801. The treaty reaffirmed and expanded earlier French gains at Campo Formio.[^140] On 26 January 1802, following the [Consulte de Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consulte_de_Lyon "Consulte de Lyon"), Napoleon was declared president of the [Italian Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Republic_\(Napoleonic\) "Italian Republic (Napoleonic)").[^141]

Bonaparte's triumph at Marengo increased his popularity and political authority. However, he still faced royalist plots and feared Jacobin influence, especially in the army. Several assassination plots, including the *[Conspiration des poignards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conspiration_des_poignards "Conspiration des poignards")* (Dagger plot) in October 1800 and the [Plot of the Rue Saint-Nicaise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_of_the_rue_Saint-Nicaise "Plot of the rue Saint-Nicaise") two months later, gave him a pretext to arrest about 100 suspected Jacobins and royalists, some of whom were shot and many others deported to penal colonies.[^142] [^143]

#### Temporary peace in Europe

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Louisiana_Purchase.png/250px-Louisiana_Purchase.png)

The 1803 Louisiana Purchase totalled 2,144,480 square kilometres (827,987 square miles), doubling the size of the United States.

After a decade of war, France and [Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland "United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland") signed the [Treaty of Amiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Amiens "Treaty of Amiens") in March 1802, bringing the [Revolutionary Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars "French Revolutionary Wars") to an end. Under the treaty, Britain agreed to withdraw from most of the colonies it had recently captured from France and her allies, and France agreed to evacuate Naples. In April, Bonaparte publicly celebrated the peace and his controversial [Concordat of 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concordat_of_1801 "Concordat of 1801") with [Pope Pius VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VII "Pope Pius VII") under which the pope recognized Bonaparte's regime and the regime recognized Catholicism as the majority religion of France. In a further step towards national reconciliation (known as "fusion"), Bonaparte offered an amnesty to most [émigrés](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89migr%C3%A9 "Émigré") who wished to return to France.[^144] [^145]

With Europe at peace and the economy recovering, Bonaparte became increasingly popular, both domestically and abroad.[^146] In May 1802, the Council of State recommended a [new plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1802_French_constitutional_referendum "1802 French constitutional referendum") asking the French people to make "Napoleon Bonaparte" consul for life. (It was the first time his first name was officially used by the regime.) [^147] About 3.6 million voted "yes" and 8,374 "no." 40–60% of eligible Frenchmen voted, the highest turnout for a [plebiscite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plebiscite "Plebiscite") since the revolution.[^148] [^149]

France had regained her overseas colonies under Amiens but did not control them all. The French National Convention had voted to abolish slavery in February 1794, but in May 1802 Bonaparte [reintroduced it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinstatement_of_slavery_by_Napoleon "Reinstatement of slavery by Napoleon") in all the recovered colonies except [Saint-Domingue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Domingue "Saint-Domingue") and [Guadeloupe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guadeloupe "Guadeloupe") which were under the control of rebel generals. A French military expedition under [Antoine Richepance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Richepance "Antoine Richepance") regained control of Guadeloupe, and slavery was reintroduced there on 16 July.[^150]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/5f_Bonabarte_Premier_consul_-_AN_XI_1802.png/250px-5f_Bonabarte_Premier_consul_-_AN_XI_1802.png)

Silver 5 francs coin depicting Napoleon as First Consul from AN XI, 1802

Saint-Domingue was the most profitable of the colonies – a major source of sugar, coffee and indigo – but was under the control of the former slave [Toussaint Louverture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toussaint_Louverture "Toussaint Louverture").[^151] Bonaparte sent the [Saint-Domingue expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Domingue_expedition "Saint-Domingue expedition") under his brother-in-law General [Charles Leclerc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Leclerc_\(general,_born_1772\) "Charles Leclerc (general, born 1772)") to retake the colony, and they landed there in February 1802 with 29,000 men. Although Toussaint was captured and sent to France in July, the expedition ultimately failed due to high rates of disease and a string of defeats against rebel commander [Jean-Jacques Dessalines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Dessalines "Jean-Jacques Dessalines"). In May 1803 Bonaparte acknowledged defeat, and the last 8,000 French troops left the island. The former slaves proclaimed the independent republic of [Haiti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haiti "Haiti") in 1804.[^152] [^153]

As war with Britain again loomed in 1803, Bonaparte realized that his American [colony of Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Territory "Louisiana Territory") would be difficult to defend.[^154] In need of funds, he agreed to the [Louisiana Purchase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase "Louisiana Purchase") with the United States, doubling the latter's size. The price was $15 million.[^155] [^156] [^157] The peace with Britain was uneasy. Britain did not evacuate [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta") as promised and protested against Bonaparte's annexation of Piedmont and his [Act of Mediation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_of_Mediation "Act of Mediation") (19 February),[^158] which established a [Swiss Confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Napoleonic_era "Switzerland in the Napoleonic era"). Neither of these territories were covered by Amiens, but they inflamed tensions significantly, as did Bonaparte's occupation of Holland and apparent ambitions in India.[^159] [^160] The dispute culminated in a declaration of war by Britain in May 1803. Bonaparte responded by reassembling the invasion camp at Boulogne and ordering the arrest of every British male between 18 and 60 years old in France and its dependencies as a [prisoner of war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner_of_war "Prisoner of war").[^161]

### French Empire

![Colored painting depicting Napoleon crowning his wife inside of a cathedral](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Jacques-Louis_David_-_The_Coronation_of_Napoleon_%281805-1807%29.jpg/250px-Jacques-Louis_David_-_The_Coronation_of_Napoleon_%281805-1807%29.jpg)

The Coronation of Napoleon by Jacques-Louis David (1804)

#### Bonaparte becomes Napoleon I

In February 1804 Bonaparte's police made a series of arrests in relation to a royalist plot to kidnap or assassinate him that involved the British government, Moreau and an unnamed [Bourbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Bourbon "House of Bourbon") prince. On the advice of his foreign minister, Talleyrand, Napoleon ordered the kidnapping of [the Duke of Enghien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Antoine,_Duke_of_Enghien "Louis Antoine, Duke of Enghien"), violating the sovereignty of [Baden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baden "Baden"). The duke was quickly executed after a secret military trial, even though there was no proof he had been involved in the plot. Enghien's kidnapping and execution infuriated royalists and monarchs throughout Europe and drew a formal protest from Russia.[^162] [^163] [^164]

Following the royalist plot, Bonaparte's supporters convinced him to convert the Consulate into a monarchy. They argued that the presence of an heir would help to secure the regime in case of his death. As well, they believed a monarchy would make Napoleon more acceptable to constitutional monarchists, and put France on the same footing as other European monarchies.[^165] [^166] [^167] On 18 May the senate proclaimed Napoleon *Emperor of the French* and approved a new constitution.[^168] The following day, Napoleon appointed 18 of his leading generals Marshals of the Empire.[^169]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/La_salle_du_Tr%C3%B4ne_%28Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Fontainebleau%29.jpg/250px-La_salle_du_Tr%C3%B4ne_%28Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Fontainebleau%29.jpg)

Napoleon's throne room at Fontainebleau

The hereditary empire was confirmed by a plebiscite in June. The official result showed 3.5 million voted "yes" and 2,569 voted "no". The yes count, however, was falsely inflated by 300,000 to 500,000 votes. The turnout, at 35%, was below the figure for the previous plebiscite.[^170] [^171] Britain, Russia, Sweden and the Ottoman Empire refused to recognize Napoleon's title. Austria, however, recognized Napoleon as Emperor of the French in return for his recognition of [Francis I as Emperor of Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor "Francis II, Holy Roman Emperor").[^172]

[Napoleon's coronation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation_of_Napoleon "Coronation of Napoleon"), with the participation of [Pope Pius VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VII "Pope Pius VII"), took place at [Notre Dame de Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Paris "Notre-Dame de Paris") on 2 December 1804. After having been anointed by the pope, Napoleon crowned himself with a replica of [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne") 's crown. He then crowned Joséphine, who became the second woman in French history, after [Marie de' Medici](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_de%27_Medici "Marie de' Medici"), to be crowned and anointed. He then swore an oath to defend the territory of the republic; to respect the Concordat, freedom of worship, political and civil liberty and the sale of nationalized lands; to raise no taxes except by law; to maintain the [Legion of Honour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legion_of_Honour "Legion of Honour"); and to govern in the interests, wellbeing and the glory of the French people.[^173]

On 17 March 1805 Napoleon declared himself [King of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Italy "King of Italy") [^174] and crowned himself with the [Iron Crown of Lombardy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Crown_of_Lombardy "Iron Crown of Lombardy") at the [Cathedral of Milan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milan_Cathedral "Milan Cathedral"). Austria saw this as a provocation because of its own territorial interests in Italy. When Napoleon incorporated [Genoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genoa "Genoa") and [Liguria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligurian_Republic "Ligurian Republic") into his empire, Austria formally protested against this violation of the Treaty of Lunéville.[^175]

#### War of the Third Coalition

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Napoleon_in_Coronation_Robes_by_Fran%C3%A7ois_G%C3%A9rard.jpg/250px-Napoleon_in_Coronation_Robes_by_Fran%C3%A7ois_G%C3%A9rard.jpg)

Napoleon in his coronation robes by François Gérard, c. 1805

By September 1805 Sweden, Russia, Austria, Naples and the Ottoman Empire had joined Britain in a coalition against France.[^176] [^177] In 1803 and 1804 Napoleon had assembled a force around [Boulogne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boulogne-sur-Mer "Boulogne-sur-Mer") for an invasion of Britain. They never invaded, but the force formed the core of Napoleon's *[Grande Armée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e "Grande Armée")*, created in August 1805.[^178] [^179] At the start, this French army had about 200,000 men organized into seven [corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corps "Corps"), artillery and cavalry reserves, and the élite Imperial Guard.[^180] [^179] By August 1805 the *Grande Armée* had grown to a force of 350,000 men,[^181] who were well equipped, well trained, and led by competent officers.[^182]

To facilitate the invasion, Napoleon planned to lure the Royal Navy from the [English Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Channel "English Channel") by a diversionary attack on the [British West Indies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_West_Indies "British West Indies").[^183] However, the plan unravelled after the British victory at the [Battle of Cape Finisterre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_Finisterre_\(1805\) "Battle of Cape Finisterre (1805)") in July 1805. French Admiral [Pierre-Charles Villeneuve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Charles_Villeneuve "Pierre-Charles Villeneuve") retreated to [Cádiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A1diz "Cádiz") instead of linking up with French naval forces at [Brest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brest,_France "Brest, France") for an attack on the English Channel.[^184] Facing a potential invasion from his continental enemies, Napoleon abandoned his invasion of England and sought to destroy the isolated Austrian armies in southern Germany before their Russian ally could arrive in force. On 25 September, 200,000 French troops began to cross the Rhine on a front of 260 km (160 mi).[^185] [^186]

![Colored painting depicting Napoleon receiving the surrender of the Austrian generals, with the opposing armies and the city of Ulm in the background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ulm_capitulation.jpg/330px-Ulm_capitulation.jpg)

Napoleon and the Grande Armée receive the surrender of Austrian General Mack after the Battle of Ulm in October 1805.

Austrian commander [Karl Mack von Leiberich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Mack_von_Leiberich "Karl Mack von Leiberich") had gathered most of the Austrian army at the fortress of [Ulm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulm "Ulm") in [Swabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swabia "Swabia"). Napoleon's army, however, moved quickly and outflanked the Austrian positions. After some minor engagements that culminated in the [Battle of Ulm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Ulm "Battle of Ulm"), Mack surrendered. With 2,000 French casualties, Napoleon had captured 60,000 Austrian soldiers through his army's rapid marching.[^187] For the French, this spectacular victory on land was soured by the decisive victory that the Royal Navy attained at the [Battle of Trafalgar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar "Battle of Trafalgar") on 21 October. After Trafalgar, the Royal Navy was never again seriously challenged by Napoleon's fleet.[^188]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/La_bataille_d%27Austerlitz._2_decembre_1805_%28Fran%C3%A7ois_G%C3%A9rard%29.jpg/500px-La_bataille_d%27Austerlitz._2_decembre_1805_%28Fran%C3%A7ois_G%C3%A9rard%29.jpg)

Napoleon at the Battle of Austerlitz, by François Gérard, 1805

French forces occupied [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna "Vienna") in November, capturing 100,000 muskets, 500 cannons, and the intact bridges across the Danube.[^189] Napoleon then sent his army north in pursuit of the allies. Tsar [Alexander I of Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Russia "Alexander I of Russia") and Francis I decided to engage Napoleon in battle, despite reservations from some of their subordinates.[^190] At the [Battle of Austerlitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Austerlitz "Battle of Austerlitz") on 2 December, Napoleon deployed his army below the Pratzen Heights. He ordered his right wing to feign retreat, enticing the allies to descend from the heights in pursuit. The French centre and left wing then captured the heights and caught the allies in a [pincer movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pincer_movement "Pincer movement"). Thousands of Russian troops fled across a frozen lake to escape the trap, and 100 to 2,000 of them drowned.[^190] [^191] About a third of the allied forces were killed, captured or wounded.[^192]

The disaster at Austerlitz led Austria to seek an armistice. By the subsequent [Treaty of Pressburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_of_Pressburg_\(1805\) "Peace of Pressburg (1805)"), signed on 26 December, Austria left the coalition, lost substantial territory to the [Kingdom of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_\(Napoleonic\) "Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)") and Bavaria, and was forced to pay an indemnity of 40 million francs. Alexander's army was granted safe passage back to Russia.[^193] [^194] Napoleon went on to say, "The battle of Austerlitz is the finest of all I have fought".[^193] [Frank McLynn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_McLynn "Frank McLynn") suggests that Napoleon was so successful at Austerlitz that he lost touch with reality, and what used to be French foreign policy became a "personal Napoleonic one".[^195] [Vincent Cronin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincent_Cronin "Vincent Cronin") disagrees, stating that Napoleon was not overly ambitious for himself, "he embodied the ambitions of thirty million Frenchmen".[^196]

#### Middle-Eastern alliances

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/The_Persian_Envoy_Mirza_Mohammed_Reza_Qazvini_Finkenstein_Castle_27_Avril_1807_by_Francois_Mulard.jpg/250px-The_Persian_Envoy_Mirza_Mohammed_Reza_Qazvini_Finkenstein_Castle_27_Avril_1807_by_Francois_Mulard.jpg)

The Iranian envoy Mirza Mohammad-Reza Qazvini meeting with Napoleon at the Finckenstein Palace in West Prussia, 27 April 1807, to sign the Treaty of Finckenstein.

Napoleon continued to entertain a grand scheme to establish a French presence in the Middle East in order to put pressure on Britain and Russia, possibly by forming an alliance with the Ottoman Empire.[^99] In February 1806, Ottoman Emperor [Selim III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selim_III "Selim III") recognized Napoleon as emperor. He also opted for an alliance with France, calling France "our sincere and natural ally".[^197] That decision brought the Ottoman Empire into a losing war against Russia and Britain. A Franco-Persian alliance was formed between Napoleon and the [Persian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qajar_dynasty "Qajar dynasty") of [Fat′h-Ali Shah Qajar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fath-Ali_Shah_Qajar "Fath-Ali Shah Qajar"). It collapsed in 1807 when France and Russia formed an unexpected alliance.[^99] In the end, Napoleon made no effective alliances in the Middle East.[^198]

#### War of the Fourth Coalition and Tilsit

After Austerlitz, Napoleon increased his political power in Europe. In 1806, he deposed the [Bourbon king of Naples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_the_Two_Sicilies "Ferdinand I of the Two Sicilies") and installed his elder brother, [Joseph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte "Joseph Bonaparte"), on the throne. He then made his younger brother, [Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Bonaparte "Louis Bonaparte"), king of Holland.[^199] On 12 July he established the [Confederation of the Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine "Confederation of the Rhine"),[^200] a collection of German states intended to serve as a buffer zone between France and Central Europe. The creation of the confederation spelled the end of the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire").[^201]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Iena.jpg/250px-Iena.jpg)

Napoleon reviewing the Imperial Guard before the Battle of Jena, 14 October 1806

Napoleon's growing influence in Germany threatened the status of Prussia as a great power and in response [Frederick William III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia "Frederick William III of Prussia") decided on war with France. Prussia and Russia signed a military alliance creating the fourth coalition against France. Prussia, however, committed a strategic blunder by declaring war when French troops were still in southern Germany and months before sufficient Russian troops could reach the front.[^202] Napoleon invaded Prussia with 180,000 troops, rapidly marching on the right bank of the River [Saale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saale "Saale"). Upon learning the whereabouts of the Prussian army, the French swung westwards thus cutting the Prussians off from Berlin and the slowly approaching Russians. At the twin [battles of Jena and Auerstedt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jena%E2%80%93Auerstedt "Battle of Jena–Auerstedt"), fought on 14 October, the French convincingly defeated the Prussians and inflicted heavy casualties. With several major commanders dead or incapacitated, the Prussian king proved incapable of effectively commanding the army, which quickly disintegrated.[^203] [^204]

In the following month, the French captured 140,000 soldiers and over 2,000 cannon. Despite their overwhelming defeat, the Prussians refused to negotiate with the French until the Russians had an opportunity to enter the fight.[^203] [^205] [^206] Following his triumph, Napoleon imposed the first elements of the [Continental System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_System "Continental System") through the [Berlin Decree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Decree "Berlin Decree") issued in November 1806. The Continental System, which prohibited European nations from trading with Britain, was widely violated throughout his reign.[^207] In the next few months, Napoleon marched against the advancing Russian armies through Poland and fought a bloody stalemate at the [Battle of Eylau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Eylau "Battle of Eylau") in February 1807.[^208] After a period of rest and consolidation on both sides, the war restarted in June with an initial struggle [at Heilsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Heilsberg "Battle of Heilsberg") that proved indecisive.[^209]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Tilsitz_1807.JPG/250px-Tilsitz_1807.JPG)

The Treaties of Tilsit: Napoleon meeting with Alexander I of Russia on a raft in the middle of the Neman River, 7 July 1807.

On 14 June Napoleon obtained an overwhelming victory over the Russians at the [Battle of Friedland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Friedland "Battle of Friedland"), inflicting casualties of up to 30% of the Russian army.[^210] The scale of their defeat convinced the Russians to make peace with the French. The two emperors began peace negotiations on 25 June at the town of [Tilsit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovetsk,_Kaliningrad_Oblast "Sovetsk, Kaliningrad Oblast") during a meeting on a raft floating in the middle of the River [Niemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neman "Neman") which separated the French and Russian troops and their respective spheres of influence.[^211] Napoleon offered Alexander relatively lenient terms—demanding that Russia join the Continental System, withdraw its forces from [Wallachia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallachia "Wallachia") and [Moldavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moldavia "Moldavia"), and hand over the [Ionian Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionian_Islands "Ionian Islands") to France. In contrast, Prussia was treated harshly. It lost half its territory and population and underwent a two-year occupation costing it about 1.4 billion francs. From former Prussian territory, Napoleon created the [Kingdom of Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia "Kingdom of Westphalia"), ruled by his young brother Jérôme, and the [Duchy of Warsaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw "Duchy of Warsaw").[^212] [^213] Prussia's humiliating treatment at Tilsit caused lasting resentment against France in that country. The treaty was also unpopular in Russia, putting pressure on Alexander to end the alliance with France. Nevertheless, the [Treaties of Tilsit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaties_of_Tilsit "Treaties of Tilsit") gave Napoleon a respite from war and allowed him to return to France, which he had not seen in over 300 days.[^212] [^214]

#### Peninsular War and Erfurt

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Joseph-Bonaparte.jpg/250px-Joseph-Bonaparte.jpg)

Portrait of Joseph Bonaparte by François Gérard, 1808, depicting Napoleon's elder brother as King of Spain

After Tilsit, Napoleon turned his attention to [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal "Kingdom of Portugal"), which was reluctant to strictly enforce the blockade against its traditional ally Britain.[^215] [^216] On 17 October 1807, 24,000 French troops under General [Jean-Andoche Junot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Andoche_Junot "Jean-Andoche Junot") [crossed the Pyrenees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasion_of_Portugal_\(1807\) "Invasion of Portugal (1807)") with Spanish consent and headed towards Portugal to enforce the blockade.[^217] Junot occupied [Lisbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon") in November; the Portuguese royal family had already fled to Brazil with the Portuguese fleet.[^218]

In March 1808 a [palace coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumult_of_Aranjuez "Tumult of Aranjuez") led to the abdication of the Spanish king, [Carlos IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Spain "Charles IV of Spain"), in favour of his son [Fernando VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain "Ferdinand VII of Spain").[^219] [^220] The following month, Napoleon summoned Carlos and Fernando to Bayonne, where in May he forced them both [to relinquish their claims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdications_of_Bayonne "Abdications of Bayonne") to the Spanish throne. Napoleon then made his brother Joseph King of Spain.[^221] By then, there were 120,000 French troops garrisoned in the peninsula [^222] [^223] and widespread Spanish opposition to the occupation and the overthrow of the Spanish Bourbons. [On 2 May an uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dos_de_Mayo_Uprising "Dos de Mayo Uprising") against the French broke out in [Madrid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madrid "Madrid") and spread throughout Spain in the following weeks. In the face of brutal French repression, the uprising developed into a sustained conflict.[^224] Joseph travelled to Madrid where he was proclaimed King of Spain on 24 July. However, following news of a French defeat by regular Spanish forces at the [Battle of Bailén](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Bail%C3%A9n "Battle of Bailén"), Joseph fled Madrid several days later.[^225] The following month, a British force landed in Portugal and on 21 August they defeated the French at the [Battle of Vimiero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Vimeiro "Battle of Vimeiro"). Under the [Convention of Cintra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Cintra "Convention of Cintra"), the French evacuated Portugal.[^226] [^227]

The defeats at Bailén and Vimiero convinced Napoleon that he had to take command of the Iberian campaign. Before leaving for Spain, he attempted to strengthen the alliance with Russia and obtain a commitment from Alexander that Russia would declare war on Austria if she attacked France. At the [Congress of Erfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_Erfurt "Congress of Erfurt") in October 1808, Napoleon and Alexander reached an agreement that recognized the Russian conquest of [Finland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland "Grand Duchy of Finland") and called upon Britain to cease its war against France.[^228] However, Alexander failed to provide a firm commitment to make war with Austria.[^229] [^230]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Capitulation_de_Madrid%2C_le_4_d%C3%A9cembre_1808.jpg/250px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Capitulation_de_Madrid%2C_le_4_d%C3%A9cembre_1808.jpg)

Napoleon Accepting the Surrender of Madrid, 4 December 1808

On 6 November Napoleon was in [Vitoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz "Vitoria-Gasteiz") and took command of 240,000 French-led troops. After a series of victories over Anglo-Spanish forces, they retook Madrid on 4 December.[^231] [^226] Napoleon then pursued a retreating British army which was eventually evacuated [at Corunna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Corunna "Battle of Corunna") in January 1809. He left for France on 17 January, leaving Joseph in command.[^232] [^233] Napoleon never returned to Spain after the 1808 campaign. In April, the British sent another army to the peninsula under [Arthur Wellesley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington "Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington"), the future [Duke of Wellington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_of_Wellington_\(title\) "Duke of Wellington (title)"). British, Portuguese and Spanish troops engaged the French in a protracted series of conflicts, while a brutal guerrilla war engulfed much of the Spanish countryside, a conflict in which atrocities were committed by both sides.[^234] [^227] Napoleon later called the Peninsular campaign, "the unlucky war \[that\] ruined me." [^235] It tied up some 300,000 French-led troops from 1808 to 1812. By 1814, the French had been driven from the peninsula, with over 150,000 casualties in the campaign.[^234] [^236]

#### War of the Fifth Coalition

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Napoleon_Wagram.jpg/250px-Napoleon_Wagram.jpg)

The Battle of Wagram by Horace Vernet, 1836

The overthrow of the Spanish Bourbons caused alarm in Austria over Napoleon's ambitions while France's military difficulties in the Peninsular encouraged Austria to go to war.[^237] [^238] In the early morning of 10 April 1809, the Austrian army crossed the [Inn River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inn_\(river\) "Inn (river)") and invaded Bavaria. The Austrian advance was disorganized, and they were unable to defeat the Bavarian army before the French could concentrate their forces.[^239] Napoleon arrived from Paris on 17 April to lead the French campaign. In the following [Battle of Eckmühl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Eckm%C3%BChl "Battle of Eckmühl") he was slightly wounded in the heel, but the Austrians were forced to retreat across the [Danube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danube "Danube"). The French occupied Vienna on 13 May, but most of the population had fled and the retreating army had destroyed all four bridges across the river.[^240]

On 21 May, the French attempted to cross the Danube, precipitating the [Battle of Aspern-Essling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Aspern-Essling "Battle of Aspern-Essling"). Both sides inflicted about 23,000 casualties on each other, and the French were forced back.[^241] The battle was reported in European capitals as a defeat for Napoleon and damaged his aura of invincibility.[^242] [^243] After six weeks of preparations, Napoleon made another attempt at crossing the Danube.[^244] In the ensuing [Battle of Wagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram "Battle of Wagram") (5–6 July) the Austrians were forced to retreat, but the French and Austrians each suffered losses of 37,000 to 39,000 killed, wounded or captured.[^245] [^246] The French caught up with the retreating Austrians at the [Battle of Znaim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Znaim "Battle of Znaim") on 10 July, and the latter signed an armistice on 12 July.[^247] In August, a British force [landed in Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walcheren_Campaign "Walcheren Campaign") but lost 4,000 men, mainly to illness, before withdrawing in December.[^248]

The [Treaty of Schönbrunn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Sch%C3%B6nbrunn "Treaty of Schönbrunn") in October 1809 was harsh for Austria which lost substantial territory and over three million subjects.[^249] France received [Carinthia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carinthia_\(Slovenia\) "Carinthia (Slovenia)"), [Carniola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carniola "Carniola"), and the [Adriatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriatic_Sea "Adriatic Sea") ports of [Trieste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trieste "Trieste") and Fiume ([Rijeka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rijeka "Rijeka")); the part of Poland annexed by Austria in the [third partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_partition_of_Poland "Third partition of Poland") in 1795, known at the time as [West Galicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Galicia "West Galicia"), was given to the Polish-ruled [Duchy of Warsaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw "Duchy of Warsaw"); and the territory of the former Archbishopric of Salzburg went to Bavaria.[^250] Austria was required to pay an indemnity of 200 million francs, and its army was reduced to 150,000 men.[^251]

#### Consolidation of the empire

![Map of Europe. French Empire shown as bigger than present day France as it included parts of present-day Netherlands and Italy.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Europe_1812_map_en.png/330px-Europe_1812_map_en.png)

The French Empire at its greatest extent in 1812: French satellite states

Napoleon's union with Joséphine had not produced a child, and he decided to secure the dynasty and strengthen its position in Europe by a strategic marriage into one of Europe's major royal houses. In November 1809, he announced his decision to divorce Joséphine, and the marriage was annulled in January 1810.[^252] Napoleon had already commenced negotiations for the marriage of Tsar Alexander's sister Anna, but the tsar responded that she was too young. Napoleon then turned to Austria, and a marriage to the Austrian emperor's daughter, Marie Louise, was quickly agreed.[^253] The marriage was formalized in a civil ceremony on 1 April and a religious service at the Louvre on the following day. The marriage to Marie Louise was widely seen as a shift in French policy towards stronger ties with Austria and away from the already strained relationship with Russia.[^254] On 20 March 1811, Marie Louise gave birth to the heir apparent, [François Charles Joseph Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_II "Napoleon II"), King of Rome.[^255]

With the annexation of the [Papal States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_States "Papal States") (May 1809, February 1810), Holland (July 1810) and the northern coastal regions of Westphalia (August 1810), mainland France further increased its territory. Napoleon now ruled about 40% of the European population either directly or indirectly through his satellite kingdoms.[^256]

#### Invasion of Russia

Tsar Alexander saw the creation of the Grand Duchy of Warsaw, Napoleon's marriage alliance with Austria, and the election of the French Marshal [Jean-Baptiste Jules Bernadotte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Jules_Bernadotte "Jean-Baptiste Jules Bernadotte") as Crown Prince of Sweden as attempts to contain Russia. In December 1810 Napoleon annexed the [Duchy of Oldenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Oldenburg "Duchy of Oldenburg"), which Alexander considered an insult as his uncle was the duke. Alexander responded by allowing neutral shipping into Russian ports and banning most French imports. Russia feared that Napoleon intended to restore the Kingdom of Poland while Napoleon suspected Russia of seeking an alliance with Britain against France.[^257] [^258]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Napoleon_in_burning_Moscow_-_Adam_Albrecht_%281841%29.jpg/250px-Napoleon_in_burning_Moscow_-_Adam_Albrecht_%281841%29.jpg)

Napoleon watching the fire of Moscow in September 1812, by Adam Albrecht (1841)

In late 1811 Napoleon began planning [an invasion of Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia "French invasion of Russia"). A Franco-Prussian alliance signed in February 1812 forced Prussia to provide 20,000 troops for the invasion, and in March Austria agreed to provide 30,000 men.[^259] [^260] Napoleon's multinational *grande armée* comprised around 450,000 frontline troops of which about a third were native French speakers. Napoleon called the invasion the "Second Polish War," but he refused to guarantee an independent Poland for fear of alienating his Austrian and Prussian allies.[^261] [^262] [^263]

On 24 June Napoleon's troops began crossing the Nieman river into [Russian Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuania "Lithuania") with the aim of luring the Russians into one or two decisive battles.[^264] The Russians retreated 320 kilometres east to the [Dvina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daugava "Daugava") river and implemented a [scorched earth policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth"), making it increasingly difficult for the French to forage food for themselves and their horses.[^265] [^266] On 18 August, Napoleon [captured Smolensk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Smolensk_\(1812\) "Battle of Smolensk (1812)") with the loss of 9,000 of his men, but the Russians were able to withdraw in good order.[^267]

The Russians, commanded by Field Marshall [Mikhail Kutuzov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Kutuzov "Mikhail Kutuzov"), [made a stand at Borodino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino "Battle of Borodino"), outside Moscow, on 7 September. The battle resulted in 44,000 Russian and 35,000 French dead, wounded or captured, in one of the bloodiest days of battle in Europe up to that time.[^268] [^269] The Russians withdrew overnight, and Napoleon later stated, "The most terrible of all my battles was the one before Moscow. The French showed themselves worthy of victory, and the Russians worthy of being invincible".[^270]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Napoleons_retreat_from_Moscow_by_Adolph_Northen.jpg/250px-Napoleons_retreat_from_Moscow_by_Adolph_Northen.jpg)

Napoleon's withdrawal from Russia, painting by Adolph Northen

The Russians retreated to [Tarutino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarutino,_Russia "Tarutino, Russia"), and [Napoleon entered Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_occupation_of_Moscow "French occupation of Moscow") on 14 September. The following evening, the city [was set on fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_of_Moscow_\(1812\) "Fire of Moscow (1812)") on the orders of Governor [Feodor Rostopchin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fyodor_Rostopchin "Fyodor Rostopchin"). Alexander, in [St Petersburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Petersburg "Saint Petersburg"), refused to negotiate a peace, and after six weeks Napoleon's army evacuated Moscow.[^271] After [capturing Maloyaroslavets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Maloyaroslavets "Battle of Maloyaroslavets") with the loss of 4,000 to 10,000 men, Napoleon retreated towards [Smolensk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smolensk "Smolensk"). The French were attacked by [Cossacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cossacks "Cossacks") and peasants and suffered from the intense cold, disease and lack of food and water. Around 40,000 to 50,000 troops reached Smolensk on 9 November, a loss of about 60,000 in three weeks. Napoleon also heard that an [attempted coup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malet_coup_of_1812 "Malet coup of 1812") by General [Claude François de Malet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Malet "Claude François de Malet") in Paris had only narrowly failed.[^272]

From Smolensk, Napoleon's army headed for [Vilnius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vilnius "Vilnius"), where there was a French garrison of 20,000. In late November, under attack from all sides by Russian forces, the *grande armée* managed to [cross the Berezina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Berezina "Battle of Berezina") river on pontoon bridges in temperatures reaching −40 °C (−40 °F). On 5 December, shortly before arriving in Vilnius, Napoleon left his disintegrating army for Paris.[^273] In the following weeks, the remnants of the *grande armée*, about 75,000 troops, crossed the Nieman into allied territory. Russian military losses in the campaign were up to 300,000, and total military deaths from both sides were up to one million.[^274]

#### War of the Sixth Coalition

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Napoleon_i_Poniatowski_Lipsk.jpg/250px-Napoleon_i_Poniatowski_Lipsk.jpg)

Napoleon and Prince Józef Poniatowski at Leipzig, painting by January Suchodolski

The French, pursued by the Russians, withdrew from most of Poland and Prussia over the winter of 1812–13 while both sides rebuilt their forces.[^275] Sweden and Prussia declared war on France in March 1813. In April Napoleon assumed command of an army of 200,000 troops [^276] [^277] and defeated the coalition at the battles of [Lützen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_\(1813\) "Battle of Lützen (1813)") and [Bautzen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Bautzen_\(1813\) "Battle of Bautzen (1813)").[^278] Britain formally joined the coalition in June followed by Austria in August,[^279] but the allies were again defeated in the [Battle of Dresden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Dresden "Battle of Dresden") in August.[^280] The coalition, however, had a growing advantage in infantry, cavalry, reserves and armaments. In the largest battle of the Napoleonic wars, the coalition was victorious at the [Battle of Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Leipzig "Battle of Leipzig") (19 October). Although coalition casualties were 54,000 men, the French lost 38,000 killed or wounded and 15,000 taken prisoner. Up to 50,000 more were lost to death, illness and desertion during the French retreat to the [Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine "Rhine").[^281] [^282]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/DelarocheNapoleon.jpg/250px-DelarocheNapoleon.jpg)

Napoleon after his abdication in Fontainebleau, 4 April 1814, by Paul Delaroche

The [Frankfurt proposals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankfurt_proposals "Frankfurt proposals") were peace terms offered by the coalition in November 1813 under which Napoleon would remain emperor but France would be reduced to its "natural frontiers." That meant that France would retain control of Belgium, Savoy and the west bank of the Rhine, while withdrawing from Spain, Holland, Italy and Germany. Napoleon did not accept the terms, and the allies crossed the Rhine into French territory on 1 January 1814.[^283] Wellington's British forces had already crossed the Pyrenees [into south-western France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_in_south-west_France_\(1814\) "Campaign in south-west France (1814)").[^284] [In north-eastern France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_in_north-east_France_\(1814\) "Campaign in north-east France (1814)"), Napoleon led about 70,000 troops against a coalition army of 200,000. After a defeat at the [Battle of La Rothière](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_La_Rothi%C3%A8re "Battle of La Rothière"), the French won a [series of victories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_Days%27_Campaign "Six Days' Campaign") in February which induced the coalition to offer peace on the basis of France's 1791 frontiers. Napoleon, however, decided to fight on.[^285] [^286]

After a series of battles in March, the allies forced Napoleon to retreat at the [Battle of Arcis-sur-Aube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Arcis-sur-Aube "Battle of Arcis-sur-Aube") (20–21 March). The coalition then [moved towards Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Paris_\(1814\) "Battle of Paris (1814)"), whose defence was under the command of Joseph Bonaparte.[^287] On 29 March, a coalition army of 200,000 began their attack on the Belleville and Montmartre heights. Empress Marie Louise fled Paris that evening with her son, the king of Rome. With an army of only 38,000 to defend the capital, Joseph authorized the French marshal [Auguste de Marmont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_de_Marmont "Auguste de Marmont") to capitulate on 31 March. The following day, the allies accepted [Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord "Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord") as head of a provisional government. On 2 April the French Senate passed the *[Acte de déchéance de l'Empereur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acte_de_d%C3%A9ch%C3%A9ance_de_l%27Empereur "Acte de déchéance de l'Empereur")*, which declared Napoleon deposed.[^288] Meanwhile, Napoleon was in [Fontainebleau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fontainebleau "Fontainebleau") with an army of 40,000 to 60,000. He contemplated a march on Paris, but on 4 April his senior commanders persuaded him to abdicate in favour of his son, with Marie Louise as regent.[^6] Tsar Alexander, however, demanded an unconditional abdication, and Napoleon reluctantly complied on 6 April.[^290] [^291] [^292] [^293]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Montfort_-_Adieux_de_Napoleon_a_la_Garde_imperiale.jpg/250px-Montfort_-_Adieux_de_Napoleon_a_la_Garde_imperiale.jpg)

Napoleon's farewell to his Imperial Guard, 20 April 1814, by Antoine-Alphonse Montfort

In his farewell address to the soldiers of the [Old Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Guard_\(France\) "Old Guard (France)") on 20 April, Napoleon said:

> "Soldiers of my Old Guard, I have come to bid you farewell. For twenty years you have accompanied me faithfully on the paths of honor and glory....With men like you, our cause was \[not\] lost, but the war would have dragged on interminably, and it would have been a civil war.... So I am sacrificing our interests to those of our country....Do not lament my fate; if I have agreed to live on, it is to serve our glory. I wish to write the history of the great deeds we have done together. Farewell, my children!" [^294]

### Exile to Elba

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Beaume_-_Napol%C3%A9on_Ier_quittant_l%27%C3%AEle_d%27Elbe_-_1836.jpg/250px-Beaume_-_Napol%C3%A9on_Ier_quittant_l%27%C3%AEle_d%27Elbe_-_1836.jpg)

Napoleon's Departure from Elba by Joseph Beaume, 1836

With the [Treaty of Fontainebleau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Fontainebleau_\(1814\) "Treaty of Fontainebleau (1814)") of 11 April 1814, the allies exiled Napoleon to [Elba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elba "Elba"), an island of 12,000 inhabitants in the Mediterranean, 10 km (6 mi) off the Tuscan coast, where they made him [sovereign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principality_of_Elba "Principality of Elba"). The following night, Napoleon attempted suicide with poison he had carried after nearly being captured by the Russians during the retreat from Moscow. Its potency had weakened with age, however, and he survived to be exiled, while his wife and son took refuge in Austria.[^295] He was conveyed to the island on [HMS *Undaunted*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Undaunted_\(1807\) "HMS Undaunted (1807)") and disembarked at [Portoferraio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portoferraio "Portoferraio") on 4 May. In the first few months on Elba, he drew up plans for administrative reforms, road and building works, and improvements to the island's mines and agriculture, but results were limited by lack of funds.[^296] [^297] [^298] When Napoleon learned that Joséphine had died in France on 29 May, he was distraught and locked himself in his room for two days.[^299]

Napoleon understood that French King [Louis XVIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XVIII "Louis XVIII") was unpopular. Realizing that his wife and son would not be joining him in exile, cut off from the allowance guaranteed to him by the Treaty of Fontainebleau, aware of rumours he was about to be banished to a remote island in the Atlantic Ocean, Napoleon escaped from Elba in the brig [*Inconstant*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_brig_Inconstant_\(1811\) "French brig Inconstant (1811)") on 26 February 1815 with about 1,000 men and a flotilla of seven vessels.[^300] [^301]

### Hundred Days

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Retour_de_Napoleon_d%27_Isle_d%27Elbe%2C_by_Charles_de_Steuben.jpg/250px-Retour_de_Napoleon_d%27_Isle_d%27Elbe%2C_by_Charles_de_Steuben.jpg)

Napoleon's Return from Elba, by Charles de Steuben, 1818

On 1 March 1815 Napoleon and his followers landed on the French mainland at [Golfe-Juan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golfe-Juan "Golfe-Juan") and headed for [Grenoble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grenoble "Grenoble") through the foothills of the Alps, taking the route now known as [Route Napoléon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Route_Napol%C3%A9on "Route Napoléon").[^300] [^302] The 5th Regiment intercepted him just south of Grenoble on 7 March. Napoleon approached the battalion alone and called to them, "Here I am. Kill your Emperor, if you wish!" The soldiers responded with, " *Vive l'empereur!*" and joined Napoleon's men.[^303] [^304] On 14 March [Marshall of the Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshal_of_the_Empire "Marshal of the Empire") [Michel Ney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Ney "Michel Ney") —who weeks earlier had boasted that he would bring Napoleon to Paris in an iron cage—joined Napoleon along with an army of 6,000.[^305]

On 13 March the powers at the [Congress of Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna "Congress of Vienna") declared Napoleon an outlaw.[^306] Four days later, Great Britain, Russia, Austria, and Prussia each pledged to put 150,000 men into the field to end his rule.[^307] Louis XVIII, however, fled Paris for Belgium in the early hours of 20 March after realizing that he did not have enough reliable troops to oppose Napoleon. Napoleon entered Paris that evening.[^308] Napoleon appointed a government and introduced constitutional changes which were approved by plebiscite in May. A [Chamber of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Representatives_\(France\) "Chamber of Representatives (France)") was also indirectly elected that month on a highly restrictive property franchise.[^309] [^310] Napoleon's priority was to raise an army to face the coalition, but the law did not allow conscription and he was only able to raise about 300,000 men, mostly raw recruits and national guards.[^311]

On 12 June Napoleon led about 124,000 men, known as the [Army of the North](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_the_North_\(France\) "Army of the North (France)"), into Belgium, aiming to drive a wedge between Wellington's army of 112,000 British, German and Dutch troops and [Gebhard Leberecht von Blücher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gebhard_Leberecht_von_Bl%C3%BCcher "Gebhard Leberecht von Blücher") 's force of 130,000 Prussians and Saxons.[^312] [^313] After engagements at the [Battle of Ligny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Ligny "Battle of Ligny") and [Battle of Quatre Bras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Quatre_Bras "Battle of Quatre Bras"), Napoleon confronted Wellington at the [Battle of Waterloo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo "Battle of Waterloo") on 18 June. Wellington's army withstood repeated attacks by the French until, late in the afternoon, Blücher's Prussians arrived in force on Napoleon's right flank. The coalition forces broke through Napoleon's lines, inflicting a devastating defeat.[^314]

Napoleon returned to Paris and found that the legislature had turned against him. Realizing that his position was untenable, [he abdicated on 22 June in favour of his son](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_I%27s_second_abdication "Napoleon I's second abdication"). He left Paris three days later and settled at Joséphine's former palace in [Château de Malmaison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Malmaison "Château de Malmaison").[^315] By 28 June, the Prussian army was at [Senlis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senlis "Senlis"), just north of Paris.[^316] When Napoleon heard that Prussian troops had orders to capture him dead or alive, he fled to [Rochefort, Charente-Maritime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rochefort,_Charente-Maritime "Rochefort, Charente-Maritime"), considering an escape to the United States. However, when he found that British ships were blockading the port, he surrendered to [Frederick Lewis Maitland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Lewis_Maitland "Frederick Lewis Maitland") on [HMS *Bellerophon*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Bellerophon_\(1786\) "HMS Bellerophon (1786)") on 15 July 1815.[^317] [^318]

## Exile on Saint Helena

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Napoleon_sainthelene.jpg/250px-Napoleon_sainthelene.jpg)

Napoleon on Saint Helena, watercolour by Franz Josef Sandmann, c. 1820

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Longwood_House_%2816311222817%29.jpg/250px-Longwood_House_%2816311222817%29.jpg)

Longwood House, Saint Helena, site of Napoleon's captivity

Napoleon was held in British custody and transferred to the island of [Saint Helena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Helena "Saint Helena") in the Atlantic Ocean, 1,870 km (1,010 nmi) from the west coast of Africa. Napoleon and 27 followers arrived at [Jamestown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamestown,_Saint_Helena "Jamestown, Saint Helena") in October 1815 on board [HMS *Northumberland*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Northumberland_\(1798\) "HMS Northumberland (1798)"). The prisoner was guarded by a garrison of 2,100 soldiers while a squadron of 10 ships continuously patrolled the waters to prevent escape.[^319] In the following years, there were rumours of escape plots, but no serious attempts were ever made.[^320]

Napoleon stayed for two months at a pavilion in [Briars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Briars,_Saint_Helena "Briars, Saint Helena") before he was moved to [Longwood House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longwood_House "Longwood House"), a 40-room wooden bungalow. The location and interior of the house were damp, windswept, rat-infested and unhealthy.[^321] [^322] *[The Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Times "The Times")* published articles insinuating the [British government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liverpool_ministry "Liverpool ministry") was trying to hasten his death. Napoleon often complained of his living conditions in letters to the island's governor [Hudson Lowe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hudson_Lowe "Hudson Lowe") [^323] while his attendants complained of "colds, [catarrhs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catarrh "Catarrh"), damp floors and poor provisions".[^324]

Napoleon insisted on imperial formality. When he held a dinner party, men were expected to wear military dress and "women \[appeared\] in evening gowns and gems. It was an explicit denial of the circumstances of his captivity".[^325] [^326] He formally received visitors, read, and dictated his memoirs and commentaries on military campaigns.[^327] He studied English under [Emmanuel, comte de Las Cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel,_comte_de_Las_Cases "Emmanuel, comte de Las Cases"), for a few months but gave up as he was poor at languages.[^328] [^329]

Napoleon circulated reports of poor treatment in the hope that public opinion would force the allies to revoke his exile on Saint Helena.[^330] Under instructions from the government, Lowe cut Napoleon's expenditure, refused to recognize him as a former emperor, and made his supporters sign a guarantee they would stay with him indefinitely.[^331] [^330] Accounts of Napoleon's treatment led in March 1817 to a debate in the [British Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom "Parliament of the United Kingdom") where [Henry Vassall-Fox, 3rd Baron Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Vassall-Fox,_3rd_Baron_Holland "Henry Vassall-Fox, 3rd Baron Holland"), made a call for a public inquiry.[^332]

In mid-1817, Napoleon's health worsened. His physician, [Barry O'Meara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_O%27Meara "Barry O'Meara"), diagnosed chronic [hepatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatitis "Hepatitis") and warned Lowe that Napoleon could die from the poor climate and lack of exercise. Lowe thought O'Meara was exaggerating and dismissed him in July 1818.[^333] In November 1818, the allies announced that Napoleon would remain a prisoner on Saint Helena for life. When he learnt the news, he became depressed and more isolated, spending longer periods in his rooms, which further undermined his health.[^334] [^335] Much of his entourage left Saint Helena, including Las Cases in December 1816, General Gaspard Gourgaud in March 1818 and [Albine de Montholon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albine_de_Montholon "Albine de Montholon") —who was possibly Napoleon's lover—in July 1819.[^336] In September 1819, two priests and the physician [François Carlo Antommarchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Carlo_Antommarchi "François Carlo Antommarchi") joined Napoleon's retinue.[^337]

### Death

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Napoleone_Bonaparte%27s_Tomb.jpg/250px-Napoleone_Bonaparte%27s_Tomb.jpg)

Napoleon's tomb at Les Invalides in Paris

Napoleon's health continued to worsen, and in March 1821 he was confined to bed. In April he wrote two wills declaring that he had been assassinated by the "English [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy "Oligarchy") ", that the Bourbons would fall, and that his son would rule France. He left his fortune to 97 legatees and asked to be buried by the Seine.[^338] On 3 May he was given the [last rites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_rites "Last rites") but could not take communion due to his illness.[^339] He died on 5 May 1821 at age 51. His last words, variously recorded by those present, were either *France, l'armée, tête d'armée, Joséphine* ("France, the army, head of the army, Joséphine"),[^340] [^341] or *qui recule*...*à la tête d'armée* ("who retreats... at the head of the army") [^342] or "France, my son, the Army." [^342]

Antommarchi and the British wrote separate autopsy reports, each concluding that Napoleon had died of internal bleeding caused by [stomach cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach_cancer "Stomach cancer"), the disease that had killed his father.[^343] [^344] A later theory, based on high concentrations of [arsenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic "Arsenic") found in samples of Napoleon's hair, held that Napoleon had died of [arsenic poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning "Arsenic poisoning"). However, subsequent studies also found high concentrations of arsenic in hair samples from Napoleon's childhood and from his son and Joséphine. Arsenic was widely used in medicines and products such as hair creams in the 19th century.[^345] [^346] A 2021 study by an international team of gastrointestinal pathologists once again concluded that Napoleon died of stomach cancer.[^344]

Napoleon was buried with military honours in the [Valley of the Geraniums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley_of_the_Tomb "Valley of the Tomb").[^347] [^340] Napoleon's heart and intestines were removed and sealed inside his coffin. [Napoleon's penis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon%27s_penis "Napoleon's penis") was allegedly removed during the autopsy and sold and exhibited. In 1840, the [British government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Melbourne_ministry "Second Melbourne ministry") gave [Louis Philippe I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Philippe_I "Louis Philippe I") permission to return Napoleon's remains to France. Napoleon's body was exhumed and found to be well preserved as it had been sealed in four coffins (two of metal and two of mahogany) and placed in a masonry tomb.[^348] On 15 December 1840, a [state funeral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_funeral "State funeral") was held in Paris with 700,000 to 1,000,000 attendees who lined the route of the funeral procession to the chapel of [Les Invalides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Invalides "Les Invalides"). The coffin was later placed in the cupola in St Jérôme's Chapel, where it remained until [Napoleon's tomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon%27s_tomb "Napoleon's tomb"), designed by [Louis Visconti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Visconti "Louis Visconti"), was completed.[^349] In 1861, during the reign of [Napoleon III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_III "Napoleon III"), his remains were entombed in a [sarcophagus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarcophagus "Sarcophagus") in the crypt under the dome at Les Invalides.[^350]

## Religion

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/France_dioceses_1801.svg/250px-France_dioceses_1801.svg.png)

Reorganisation of the religious geography: France is divided into 59 dioceses and 10 ecclesiastical provinces.

### Religious beliefs

Napoleon was baptized in [Ajaccio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajaccio "Ajaccio") on 21 July 1771 and raised a Roman Catholic. He began to question his faith at age 13 while at Brienne.[^351] Biographers have variously described him from that time as a [deist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deism "Deism"), a follower of [Jean-Jacques Rousseau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau "Jean-Jacques Rousseau") 's "natural religion" or a believer in destiny. He consistently expressed his belief in a God or creator.[^352]

He understood the power of organized religion in social and political affairs and later sought to use it to support his regime.[^353] [^354] His attitude to religion is often described as utilitarian.[^355] [^356] In 1800 he stated, "it was by making myself a Catholic that I won the war in the Vendée, by making myself a Moslem that I established myself in Egypt, by making myself an [ultramontane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultramontanism "Ultramontanism") that I turned men's hearts towards me in Italy. If I were to govern a nation of Jews I would rebuild the [Temple of Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem "Temple in Jerusalem")." [^355]

Napoleon had a [civil marriage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_marriage "Civil marriage") with Joséphine in 1796 and, at the pope's insistence, a private religious ceremony with her the day before his coronation as emperor in 1804. This marriage was annulled by tribunals under Napoleon's control in January 1810.[^357] In April 1810, Napoleon married Austrian princess Marie Louise in a Catholic ceremony. Napoleon was [excommunicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excommunicated "Excommunicated") by the pope through the bull *[Quum memoranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quum_memoranda "Quum memoranda")* in 1809.[^358] His will in 1821 stated, "I die in the Apostolical Roman religion, in the bosom of which I was born, more than fifty years since." [^359] Napoleon read the [Quran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quran "Quran") in translation and had an interest in [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") and the [Orient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orient "Orient").[^360] He also defended [Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad "Muhammad") ("a great man") against [Voltaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaire "Voltaire") 's *[Mahomet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahomet_\(play\) "Mahomet (play)")*.[^361]

### Concordat

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/FrenchChurchOathConcordat.jpg/250px-FrenchChurchOathConcordat.jpg)

Leaders of the Catholic Church taking the civil oath required by the Concordat of 1801

Seeking national reconciliation between revolutionaries and Catholics, Napoleon and [Pope Pius VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VII "Pope Pius VII") agreed to the [Concordat of 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concordat_of_1801 "Concordat of 1801"). The agreement recognized the Catholic Church as the majority church of France and in return the Church recognized Napoleon's regime, undercutting much of the ground from royalists. The Concordat confirmed the seizure of Church lands and endowments during the revolution, but reintroduced state salaries for the clergy. The government also controlled the nomination of bishops for investiture by the pope. Bishops and other clergy were required to swear an oath of loyalty to the regime.[^362] [^363] [^364]

When the Concordat was published on 8 April 1802, Napoleon presented another set of laws called the [Organic Articles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_Articles "Organic Articles") which further increased state control over the French Church.[^362] Similar arrangements were made with the Church in territories controlled by Napoleon, especially in Italy and Germany.[^365]

### Arrest of Pope Pius VII

Napoleon progressively occupied and annexed the Papal States from 1805. When he annexed Rome in May 1809, the pope excommunicated him the following month. In July, French officials arrested the pope in the Vatican and exiled him to [Savona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savona "Savona"). In 1812 the pontiff was transferred to the [Palace of Fontainebleau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace_of_Fontainebleau "Palace of Fontainebleau") in France.[^366] In January 1813, Napoleon pressured the pope to sign a new "Concordat of Fontainebleau" which was soon repudiated by the pontiff. The pope was not released until 1814.[^358]

### Religious emancipation

In February 1795, the National Convention proclaimed religious equality for France's [Protestant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism "Protestantism") churches and other religions. In April 1802, Napoleon published laws increasing state control of [Calvinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformed_Christianity "Reformed Christianity") congregations and [Lutheran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism") directories, with their pastors to be paid by the state.[^367] With Napoleon's military victories, formal religious equality and civil rights for religious minorities spread to the conquered territories and [satellite states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_state "Satellite state"), although their implementation varied with the local authorities.[^368]

[Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") in France had been granted full civil rights in September 1791 and religious equality in 1795. The revolutionary and Napoleonic regimes abolished Jewish ghettoes in the territories they conquered.[^369] Napoleon wished to assimilate Jews into French society and convened an assembly of Jewish notables in 1806 to that end. In 1807, he summoned a [Sanhedrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanhedrin "Sanhedrin") to adapt the [law of Moses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_Moses "Law of Moses") to those of the empire. An imperial decree of March 1808 organized Jewish worship into consistories, limited usury and encouraged Jews to adopt a family name, intermarriage, and civil marriage and divorce.[^10] [^369] Jews, however, were still subject to discrimination in many parts of the empire and satellite states.[^368]

## Personality

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Napoleon_visiting_the_Tribunat_%28Palais_Royal%29_in_1807.jpg/250px-Napoleon_visiting_the_Tribunat_%28Palais_Royal%29_in_1807.jpg)

Napoleon visiting the Tribunat

[Pieter Geyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pieter_Geyl "Pieter Geyl") wrote in 1947, "It is impossible that two historians, especially two historians living in different periods, should see any historical personality in the same light." [^370] There is no dispute that Napoleon was ambitious, although commentators disagree on whether his ambition was mostly for his own power and glory or for the welfare of France.[^371] [^372] [^373] Historians agree that Napoleon was highly intelligent with an excellent memory [^374] [^375] [^376] and was a superior organizer who could work efficiently for long hours.[^375] [^377] In battle, he could rapidly dictate a series of complex commands to his subordinates, keeping in mind where major units were expected to be at each future point.[^378]

He was an inspiring leader who could obtain the best from his soldiers and subordinates.[^379] Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington, said his presence on the battlefield was worth 40,000 soldiers.[^380] [^381] He could charm people when he needed to but could also publicly humiliate them and was known for his rages when his plans were frustrated.[^382] [^372] [^383] [^384] The historian [Frank McLynn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_McLynn "Frank McLynn") sees him as a [misogynist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misogynist "Misogynist") with a cruel streak which he often inflicted on women, children and animals.[^385]

There is debate over whether Napoleon was an outsider who never felt at home in France or with other people.[^386] [Hippolyte Taine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Taine "Hippolyte Taine") said Napoleon saw others only as instruments and was cut off from feelings of admiration, sympathy or pity. Arthur Lévy replied that Napoleon genuinely loved Joséphine and often showed humanity and compassion to his enemies or those who had let him down. He had the normal middle class virtues and understood the common man.[^387]

Historians are divided over whether Napoleon was consistently ruthless when his power was threatened or surprisingly indulgent in some cases. Those arguing for a ruthless personality point to episodes such as his violent suppression of revolts in France and conquered territories,[^388] his execution of the [Duc d'Enghien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Antoine,_Duke_of_Enghien "Louis Antoine, Duke of Enghien") and plotters against his rule,[^16] [^389] and his massacre of Turkish prisoners of war in Syria in 1799.[^383] [^110] Others point to his mild treatment of disloyal subordinates such as [Bernadotte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_XIV_John "Charles XIV John"), Talleyrand, and Fouché.[^383]

Many historians see Napoleon as pragmatic and a realist, at least in the early years of his rule.[^390] [^391] [^392] He was not driven by ideology and promoted capable men irrespective of their political and social background, as long as they were loyal.[^393] [^394] As an expert in military matters, he valued technical expertise and listened to the advice of experts in other fields.[^393] However, there is a consensus that once he dominated Europe he became more intolerant of other views and surrounded himself with " [yes men](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sycophancy "Sycophancy") ".[^395] [^396] Towards the end of his reign he lost his realism and ability to compromise.[^397] [^398]

Some historians talk of Napoleon's dual nature: a rationalist with a strong romantic streak.[^399] [^400] He took a team of scholars, artists and engineers with him to Egypt in order to scientifically study the country's culture and history, but at the same time was struck by romantic "orientalism". "I was full of dreams," he stated. "I saw myself founding a religion, marching into Asia, riding an elephant, a turban on my head and in my hand a new Koran that I would have composed to suit my need." [^401]

Napoleon was superstitious. He believed in [omens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omen "Omen"), [numerology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerology "Numerology"), fate and lucky stars, and always asked of his generals: "is he lucky?" [^402] Dwyer states that Napoleon's victories at Austerlitz and Jena in 1805–06 left him even more certain of his destiny and invincibility.[^403] "I am of the race that founds empires", he once boasted, deeming himself an heir to the Ancient Romans.[^404]

Various psychologists have attempted to explain Napoleon's personality. [Alfred Adler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Adler "Alfred Adler") cites Napoleon to describe an [inferiority complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inferiority_complex "Inferiority complex") in which short people adopt over-aggressive behaviour to compensate for lack of height; this inspired the term *[Napoleon complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_complex "Napoleon complex")*.[^405] Adler, [Erich Fromm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Fromm "Erich Fromm") and [Wilhelm Reich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Reich "Wilhelm Reich") ascribe his nervous energy to [sexual dysfunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_dysfunction "Sexual dysfunction").[^406] Harold T. Parker speculated that rivalry with his older brother and bullying when he moved to France led him to develop an inferiority complex which made him domineering.[^407]

## Appearance and image

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Napoleon_in_1806.PNG/250px-Napoleon_in_1806.PNG)

Napoleon is often represented in his green colonel uniform of the Chasseur à Cheval of the Imperial Guard, the regiment that often served as his personal escort, with a large bicorne and a hand-in-waistcoat gesture.

In his youth, Bonaparte was consistently described as small and thin. Johann Ludwig Wurstemberger, who accompanied him in 1797 and 1798, notes "Bonaparte was rather slight and emaciated-looking; his face, too, was very thin, with a dark complexion... his black, unpowdered hair hung down evenly over both shoulders", but that, despite his slight and unkempt appearance, "his looks and expression were earnest and powerful." [^408]

The English painter [Joseph Farington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Farington "Joseph Farington"), who met him in 1802, said Bonaparte's eyes were "lighter, and more of a grey, than I should have expected from his complexion", "his person is below middle size", and "his general aspect was milder than I had before thought it." [^409] In his later years Napoleon gained weight and had a sallow complexion. The novelist [Paul de Kock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Paul_de_Kock "Charles Paul de Kock"), who saw him in 1811, called Napoleon "yellow, obese, and bloated".[^410] He is often portrayed wearing a large [bicorne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicorne "Bicorne") hat—sideways—with a [hand-in-waistcoat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hand-in-waistcoat "Hand-in-waistcoat") gesture—a reference to the painting produced in 1812 by [Jacques-Louis David](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David "Jacques-Louis David").[^411]

During the Napoleonic Wars, the British press depicted Napoleon as a dangerous [tyrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant"), poised to invade. A nursery rhyme warned children that he ate naughty people; the " [bogeyman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bogeyman "Bogeyman") ".[^412] He was mocked as a short-tempered small man and was nicknamed "Little Boney in a strong fit".[^413] In fact, at about 168 cm (5 ft 6 in), he was of average height.[^414] [^415]

## Reforms

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Debret_-_Premiere_distribution_des_decorations_de_la_Legion_d%27honneur.jpg/250px-Debret_-_Premiere_distribution_des_decorations_de_la_Legion_d%27honneur.jpg)

First remittance of the Legion of Honour, 15 July 1804, at Saint-Louis des Invalides, by Jean-Baptiste Debret (1812)

Napoleon instituted numerous reforms, many of which had a lasting influence on France, Europe, and the world. He reformed the French administration, codified French law, implemented a new education system, and established the first French central bank, the [Banque de France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_France "Bank of France").[^416] He negotiated the Concordat of 1801 with the Catholic Church, which sought to reconcile the majority Catholic population to his regime. It was presented alongside the Organic Articles, which regulated public worship in France. He also implemented civil and religious equality for Protestants and Jews.[^417] In May 1802 he instituted the [Legion of Honour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legion_of_Honour "Legion of Honour") to encourage civilian and military achievements. The order is still the highest decoration in France.[^418] [^419] He introduced three French constitutions culminating in the reintroduction of a hereditary monarchy and nobility.[^420]

### Administration

Napoleon introduced a series of centralizing administrative reforms soon after taking power. In 1800, he established prefects appointed to run France's regional departments, sub-prefects to run districts and mayors to run towns. Local representative bodies were retained, but their powers were reduced and indirect elections with a high property qualification replaced direct elections.[^421] Real power in the regions was now in the hands of the prefects who were judged by how they met the main priorities of Napoleon's government: efficient administration, law and order, stimulating the local economy, gathering votes for plebiscites, conscripting soldiers and provisioning the army.[^422] [^423]

An enduring reform was the foundation, in December 1799, of the [Council of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conseil_d%27%C3%89tat_\(France\) "Conseil d'État (France)"), an advisory body of experts which could also draft laws for submission to the legislative body. Napoleon drew many of his ministers and ambassadors from the council. It was the council which undertook the codification of French law.[^424]

After several attempts by revolutionary governments, Napoleon officially introduced the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system") in France in 1801, and it was spread through western Europe by his armies.[^425] [^426] The system was unpopular in some circles, so in 1812 he introduced a compromise system in the retail trade called the *[mesures usuelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesures_usuelles "Mesures usuelles")* (traditional units of measurement).[^427] In December 1805, Napoleon abolished the revolutionary calendar, with its ten-day week, which had been introduced in 1793.[^428]

### Napoleonic Code

![Page of French writing](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Code_Civil_1804.png/250px-Code_Civil_1804.png)

First page of the 1804 original edition of the Code Civil

Napoleon's [civil code of laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_code "Civil code"), known from 1807 as the [Napoleonic Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Code "Napoleonic Code"), was implemented in March 1804. It was prepared by committees of legal experts under the supervision of [Jean Jacques Régis de Cambacérès](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques-R%C3%A9gis_de_Cambac%C3%A9r%C3%A8s "Jean-Jacques-Régis de Cambacérès"), the *Second Consul*. Napoleon participated actively in the sessions of the Council of State that revised the drafts. The code introduced a clearly written and accessible set of national laws to replace the various regional and customary law systems that had operated in France.[^429]

The civil code entrenched the principles of equality before the law, religious toleration, secure property rights, equal inheritance for all legitimate children, and the abolition of the vestiges of feudalism. However, it also reduced the rights of women and children and severely restricted the grounds for divorce.[^430]

A criminal code was promulgated in 1808, and eventually seven codes of law were produced under Napoleon.[^431] The Napoleonic Code was carried by Napoleon's armies across Europe and influenced the law in many parts of the world. [Alfred Cobban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Cobban "Alfred Cobban") describes it as, "the most effective agency for the propagation of the basic principles of the French Revolution." [^432]

### Warfare

![Photo of a grey and phosphorous-coloured equestrian statue. Napoleon is seated on the horse, which is rearing up, he looks forward with his right hand raised and pointing forward; his left hand holds the reins.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Napoleon_a_Cherbourg_bordercropped.jpg/250px-Napoleon_a_Cherbourg_bordercropped.jpg)

Statue in Cherbourg-Octeville unveiled by Napoleon III in 1858. Napoleon I strengthened the town's defences to prevent British naval incursions.

In the field of [military organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_organization "Military organization"), Napoleon borrowed from previous theorists such as [Jacques Antoine Hippolyte, Comte de Guibert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Antoine_Hippolyte,_Comte_de_Guibert "Jacques Antoine Hippolyte, Comte de Guibert"), and from the reforms of preceding French governments, and then developed what was already in place. He continued the revolutionary policies of conscription and promotion based primarily on merit.[^433] [^434]

[Corps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corps "Corps") replaced divisions as the largest army units, [mobile artillery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-propelled_artillery "Self-propelled artillery") was integrated into reserve batteries, the staff system became more fluid, and cavalry returned as an important formation in French military doctrine. These methods are now referred to as essential features of Napoleonic warfare.[^433]

Napoleon was regarded by the influential military theorist [Carl von Clausewitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_von_Clausewitz "Carl von Clausewitz") as a genius in the art of war, and many historians rank him as a great military commander.[^433] Wellington considers him the greatest military commander of all time,[^435] and [Henry Vassall-Fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Vassall-Fox,_3rd_Baron_Holland "Henry Vassall-Fox, 3rd Baron Holland") calls him "the greatest statesman and the ablest general of ancient or modern times".[^436] Cobban states that he showed his genius in moving troops quickly and concentrating them on strategic points.[^437] His principles were to keep his forces united, keep no weak point unguarded, seize important points quickly, and seize his chance.[^438] Owen Connelly, however, states, "Napoleon's personal tactics defy analysis." He used his intuition, engaged his troops, and reacted to what developed.[^439]

Napoleon was an aggressive commander with a preference for the offensive.[^440] Under Napoleon, the focus shifted towards destroying enemy armies rather than simply outmanoeuvering them. Wars became more costly and decisive as invasions of enemy territory occurred on larger fronts. The political cost of war also increased, as defeat for a European power meant more than just losing isolated territories. Peace terms were often punitive, sometimes involving regime change, which intensified the trend towards [total war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_war "Total war") since the revolutionary era.[^433] [^441]

### Education

Napoleon's educational reforms laid the foundation of a modern system of secondary and tertiary education in France and throughout much of Europe.[^442] He synthesized academic elements from the *[Ancien Régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime "Ancien Régime")*, [The Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment"), and the French Revolution.[^443] His education laws of 1802 left most primary education in the hands of religious or communal schools which taught basic literacy and numeracy for a minority of the population.[^444] He abolished the revolutionary central schools and replaced them with secondary schools and elite lycées where the curriculum was based on reading, writing, mathematics, Latin, natural history, classics, and ancient history.[^445]

He retained the revolutionary higher education system, with *grandes écoles* in professions including law, medicine, pharmacy, engineering and school teaching. He introduced *grandes écoles* in history and geography, but opposed one in literature because it was not vocational. He also founded the [military academy of Saint Cyr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_sp%C3%A9ciale_militaire_de_Saint-Cyr "École spéciale militaire de Saint-Cyr").[^446] He promoted the advanced centres, such as the [École Polytechnique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique "École Polytechnique"), that provided both military expertise and advanced research in science.[^447]

In 1808, he founded the Imperial University, a supervisory body with control over curriculum and discipline. The following year he introduced the baccalaureate.[^448] The system was designed to produce the efficient bureaucrats, technicians, professionals and military officers that the Napoleonic state required. It outperformed its European counterparts, many of which borrowed from the French system.[^449] Female education, in contrast, was designed to be practical and religious, based on home science, the catechism, basic literacy and numeracy, and enough science to eradicate superstition.[^450]

### Nobility and honours

In May 1802, Bonaparte created the Legion of Honour whose members would be military personnel and civilians with distinguished service to the state. The institution was unpopular with republicans, and the measure passed by 14 votes to 10 in the Council of State.[^451] The Legion of Honour became an order of chivalry after the empire was proclaimed in 1804. In August 1806, Napoleon created an hereditary imperial nobility including princes, dukes, counts, barons and knights. Eventually the empire had over 3,000 nobles and more than 30,000 members of the Legion of Honour.[^452] [^453]

## Memory and evaluation

### Criticism

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/El_Tres_de_Mayo%2C_by_Francisco_de_Goya%2C_from_Prado_thin_black_margin.jpg/250px-El_Tres_de_Mayo%2C_by_Francisco_de_Goya%2C_from_Prado_thin_black_margin.jpg)

The Third of May 1808 by Francisco Goya, showing Spanish resisters being executed by French troops

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Plate_L_from_%27An_Historical_Account_of_the_Campaign_in_the_Netherlands%27_by_William_Mudford_%281817%29.jpg/250px-Plate_L_from_%27An_Historical_Account_of_the_Campaign_in_the_Netherlands%27_by_William_Mudford_%281817%29.jpg)

A mass grave of soldiers killed at the Battle of Waterloo

There is debate over whether Napoleon was "an [enlightened despot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enlightened_absolutism "Enlightened absolutism") who laid the foundations of modern Europe" or "a [megalomaniac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narcissistic_personality_disorder "Narcissistic personality disorder") who wrought greater misery than any man before the coming of [Hitler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hitler "Adolf Hitler") ".[^454] He was compared to Adolf Hitler by [Pieter Geyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pieter_Geyl "Pieter Geyl") in 1947 [^455] and [Claude Ribbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Ribbe "Claude Ribbe") in 2005.[^456] Most modern critics of Napoleon, however, reject the Hitler comparison, arguing that Napoleon did not commit genocide and did not engage in the mass murder and imprisonment of his political opponents.[^457] [^458] Nevertheless, [David A. Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_A._Bell "David A. Bell") and McLynn condemn his killing of 3,000–5,000 Turkish prisoners of war in Syria.[^110] [^111]

Historians have argued that his expansionist foreign policy was a major factor in the Napoleonic wars,[^459] [^460] which cost six million lives and caused economic disruption for a generation.[^461] [^462] McLynn and [Correlli Barnett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlli_Barnett "Correlli Barnett") suggest that Napoleon's reputation as a military genius is exaggerated.[^463] [^464] Cobban and Susan P. Conner argue that Napoleon had insufficient regard for the lives of his soldiers and that his battle tactics led to excessive casualties.[^465] [^466]

Critics also cite Napoleon's exploitation of conquered territories.[^464] To finance his wars, Napoleon increased taxes and levies of troops from annexed territories and satellite states.[^467] [^468] He also introduced discriminatory tariff policies which promoted French trade at the expense of allies and satellite states.[^469] He institutionalized plunder: French museums contain art stolen by Napoleon's forces from across Europe. Artefacts were brought to the [Musée du Louvre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louvre "Louvre") for a grand central museum; an example which would later be followed by others.[^470]

Many historians have criticized Napoleon's authoritarian rule, especially after 1807, which included censorship, the closure of independent newspapers, the bypassing of direct elections and representative government, the dismissal of judges showing independence, and the exile of critics of the regime.[^14] [^471] [^16] Historians also blame Napoleon for reducing the civil rights of women, children and people of colour, and reintroducing the legal penalties of [civil death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_death "Civil death") and confiscation of property.[^472] [^471] [^473] His reintroduction of an hereditary monarchy and nobility remains controversial.[^451] [^474] His role in the Haitian Revolution and decision to reinstate slavery in France's colonies in the Caribbean and Indian Ocean adversely affect his reputation.[^475] [^476]

### Propaganda and memory

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Napoleon%27s_exile_to_Elba3.jpg/250px-Napoleon%27s_exile_to_Elba3.jpg)

1814 English caricature of Napoleon being exiled to Elba: the ex-emperor is riding a donkey backwards while holding a broken sword.

[Napoleon's use of propaganda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_propaganda "Napoleonic propaganda") contributed to his rise to power, legitimated his regime, and established his image for posterity. Strict censorship and control of the [press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_media "Mass media"), books, theatre, and art were part of his propaganda scheme, aimed at portraying him as bringing peace and stability to France. Propaganda focused on his role first as a general then as a civil leader and emperor. He fostered a relationship with artists, commissioning and controlling different forms of art to suit his propaganda goals.[^477] Napoleonic propaganda survived his exile to Saint Helena. Las Cases, who was with Napoleon in exile, published *[The Memorial of Saint Helena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Memorial_of_Saint_Helena "The Memorial of Saint Helena")* in 1822, creating a legend of Napoleon as a liberal, visionary proponent of European unification, deposed by reactionary elements of the *[ancien régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_r%C3%A9gime "Ancien régime")*.[^478] [^479]

Napoleon remained a central figure in the romantic art and literature of the 1820s and 1830s.[^480] The Napoleonic legend played a key role in collective political defiance of the Bourbon restoration monarchy in 1815–1830. People from different walks of life and areas of France, particularly Napoleonic veterans, drew on the Napoleonic legacy and its connections with the ideals of the French Revolution.[^481] The defiance manifested itself in seditious materials, displaying the tricolour and rosettes. There were also subversive activities celebrating anniversaries of Napoleon's life and reign and disrupting royal celebrations.[^481]

Bell sees the return of Napoleon's remains to France in 1840 as an attempt by Louis-Phillipe to prop up his unpopular regime by associating it with Napoleon, and that the regime of Napoleon III was only possible with the continued resonance of the Napoleonic legend.[^482] Venita Datta argues that following the collapse of militaristic [Boulangism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Ernest_Boulanger "Georges Ernest Boulanger") in the late 1880s, the Napoleonic legend was divorced from party politics and revived in popular culture. Writers and critics of the *[Belle Époque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque "Belle Époque")* exploited the Napoleonic legend for diverse political and cultural ends.[^483] In the 21st century, Napoleon appears regularly in popular fiction, drama and advertising. Napoleon and his era remain major topics of historical research with a sharp increase in historical books, articles and symposia during the bicentenary years of 1999 to 2015.[^484] [^485]

### Long-term influence outside France

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Flickr_-_USCapitol_-_Napoleon_I_%281769-1821%29.jpg/250px-Flickr_-_USCapitol_-_Napoleon_I_%281769-1821%29.jpg)

Bas-relief of Napoleon in the chamber of the United States House of Representatives

Napoleon was responsible for spreading many of the values of the French Revolution to other countries, especially through the Napoleonic Code.[^486] After the fall of Napoleon, it continued to influence the law in western Europe and other parts of the world including Latin America, the Dominican Republic, Louisiana and Quebec.[^487]

Napoleon's regime abolished remnants of feudalism in the lands he conquered and in his satellite states. He liberalized [property laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_law "Property law"), ended [manorialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manorialism "Manorialism"), abolished the [guild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_guild "Medieval guild") of merchants and craftsmen to facilitate entrepreneurship, legalized divorce, closed the Jewish [ghettos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghetto "Ghetto") and ended the [Spanish Inquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition "Spanish Inquisition"). The power of church courts and religious authority was sharply reduced and [equality before the law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_before_the_law "Equality before the law") was proclaimed for all men.[^488]

Napoleon reorganized what had been the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire"), made up of about three hundred *[Kleinstaaterei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleinstaaterei "Kleinstaaterei")*, into a more streamlined forty-state [Confederation of the Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine "Confederation of the Rhine"); this helped promote the [German Confederation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Confederation "German Confederation") and the [unification of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unification_of_Germany "Unification of Germany") in 1871, as it sparked a new wave of [German nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_nationalism "German nationalism") that opposed the French intervention.[^489] The movement toward [Italian unification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_unification "Italian unification") was similarly sparked by Napoleonic rule.[^490] These changes contributed to the development of nationalism and the [nation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state "Nation state").[^491]

The Napoleonic invasion of Spain and ousting of the Spanish Bourbon monarchy had a significant effect on [Spanish America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispanic_America "Hispanic America"). Many local elites sought to rule in the name of [Ferdinand VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_VII "Ferdinand VII"), whom they considered the legitimate monarch. Napoleon indirectly began the process of [Latin American independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence "Spanish American wars of independence") when the power vacuum was filled by local political leaders such as [Simón Bolívar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar "Simón Bolívar") and [José de San Martín](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn "José de San Martín"). Such leaders embraced nationalistic sentiments influenced by French nationalism and led successful independence movements in Latin America.[^492] [^493] Napoleon's reputation is generally favourable in Poland, which is the only country in the world to evoke him in its national anthem, [Poland Is Not Yet Lost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland_Is_Not_Yet_Lost "Poland Is Not Yet Lost").[^494]

## Children

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Fran%C3%A7ois_Pascal_Simon_G%C3%A9rard_004b.jpg/250px-Fran%C3%A7ois_Pascal_Simon_G%C3%A9rard_004b.jpg)

Empress Marie Louise and her son Napoleon, by François Gérard, 1813

Napoleon married Joséphine in 1796, but the marriage produced no children.[^495] In 1806 he adopted his step-son [Eugène de Beauharnais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_de_Beauharnais "Eugène de Beauharnais") and his second cousin [Stéphanie de Beauharnais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A9phanie_de_Beauharnais "Stéphanie de Beauharnais"), and he arranged dynastic marriages for them.[^496]

Napoleon's marriage to Marie Louise produced one child, Napoleon Francis Joseph Charles ([Napoleon II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_II "Napoleon II")), known from birth as the [King of Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_the_Romans "King of the Romans"). When Napoleon abdicated in 1815 he named his son his successor as "Napoleon II", but the allies refused to recognize him. He was awarded the title of the Duke of Reichstadt in 1818 and died of [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis") aged 21, with no children.[^497] [^498]

Napoleon acknowledged one illegitimate son: [Charles Léon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_L%C3%A9on "Charles Léon") by [Eléonore Denuelle de La Plaigne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El%C3%A9onore_Denuelle_de_La_Plaigne "Eléonore Denuelle de La Plaigne").[^499] [^500] [Alexandre Colonna-Walewski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandre_Colonna-Walewski "Alexandre Colonna-Walewski"), the son of his Polish mistress [Maria Walewska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Walewska "Maria Walewska"), was also widely known to be his child,[^495] as DNA evidence has confirmed.[^501] He may have had further illegitimate offspring.[^502]
